Taxonomic Distinctions in Arabic Verb Morphology: An Exhaustive Analysis of Derivational Patterns and Temporal Frameworks
1. Introduction to Arabic Morphological Taxonomy
The architectural framework of Arabic morphology, known classically as ‘Ilm al-Sarf or al-Tasrif, is characterized by an exquisitely systematic mathematical structure governing the formation, derivation, and inflection of words. Unlike concatenative languages that rely predominantly on linear affixation, the Arabic language utilizes a non-concatenative root-and-pattern system. Within this highly structured environment, the specific domain of verbs (al-Af‘al) presents unique taxonomic challenges for grammarians, linguists, and modern database architects. Taxonomists frequently encounter overlapping categorical paradigms, the most persistent of which is the distinction between derivational verb forms or patterns (Abwab al-Fi‘l or Abniyat al-Fi‘l) and inflectional verb tenses (Azminat al-Fi‘l).
The central analytical problem addressed in this comprehensive report is whether the classical morphological classification of "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" (deriving verbs from one another) is genuinely distinct from "أزمنة الفعل" (verb tenses). A common misconception assumes that the former governs the horizontal expansion of verbs—for instance, the derivation of Form II or Form III from the base Form I root—while the latter governs the vertical axis of time. However, a rigorous reassessment of classical morphological texts, semantic paradigms, and structural linguistics reveals a critical divergence between modern terminological assumptions and classical definitions.
To provide a definitive answer regarding the structuring of these taxonomies, this report will conduct a multi-layered analysis of classical treatises, including Al-Shafiya by Ibn al-Hajib, Shadha al-Arf fi Fann al-Sarf by Ahmad al-Hamlawi, Matn al-Bina, and Nuzhat al-Tarf. By deconstructing the epistemology of Tasrif, outlining the precise mechanics of the horizontal and vertical derivational axes, and mapping these classical concepts to modern computational linguistics, this report will establish a definitive structural recommendation for morphological ontologies, ensuring that scholarly excerpts can be accurately and cleanly classified.
2. Epistemological and Historical Foundations of Tasrif
To systematically untangle the overlapping branches of verb derivation, it is imperative to first establish the classical epistemological parameters of Tasrif. The linguistic term, derived from the Arabic root sad-ra-fa (indicating turning, changing, averting, or transforming), essentially implies systemic modification.1 The science of morphology, widely considered the mother of Arabic sciences (Umm al-‘Ulum al-‘Arabiyya), operates precisely through this concept of structural transformation.1
2.1 The Bipartite Nature of Morphological Transformation
In classical Arabic linguistic theory, the science of morphology serves two distinct functional purposes. These purposes were articulated early in the grammatical tradition and prominently summarized by authorities such as Ibn Usfur, whose frameworks heavily influenced later texts.2 The discipline is bifurcated into two primary domains, which dictate how words are organized in classical texts:
The first domain is Semantic Derivation (التصريف المعنوي or التصريف الأول). This represents the intentional transformation of a single root or base word into various different morphological templates to yield new, distinct, but semantically related meanings.1 This domain encompasses both horizontal derivation and vertical inflection. The transformation of the base verb daraba (ضَرَبَ - he struck) into the augmented form darraba (ضَرَّبَ - he struck intensely), the reciprocal form tadaraba (تَضَارَبَ - they struck each other), or the active participle darib (ضَارِب - striker) falls strictly under this category.2 In all these instances, the morphological shift serves a deliberate semantic purpose, introducing nuances of causation, intensity, participation, or agency.
The second domain is Phonetic and Structural Modification (التصريف اللفظي). This involves mechanical changes applied to the structure of a word to alleviate phonetic heaviness, avoid impermissible consonant clusters (Iltiqa' al-Sakinayn), or conform to established phonological rules, without altering the underlying semantic meaning.4 This encompasses phenomena such as assimilation (Idgham), substitution (Ibdal), deletion (Hadhf), and weak-letter transformation (I‘lal). For example, the transformation of the theoretical root qawala (قَوَلَ) into the realized form qala (قَالَ), or the shift from rumiya to rama (رَمَى), involves no semantic shift whatsoever; the meaning remains identical, but the structure is altered for phonetic fluidity.2
2.2 The Root of Derivation: The Kufan and Basran Paradigms
Any attempt to classify the concept of "deriving verbs from one another" must contend with the historical debate regarding the ultimate point of origin. Classical morphology is profoundly shaped by the historic schism between the Basran and Kufan grammatical schools. The Basran school posits that the verbal noun (Masdar) is the ultimate and absolute root (Asl) of all derivation, arguing that it represents the pure event or action unconstrained by time.1 According to this model, the past tense verb is the first derivative of the Masdar.
Conversely, the Kufan school posits that the past tense verb (Fi‘l Madi) is the primary root, arguing that it is the simplest verbal form and that the Masdar often contains augmentations or phonetic complexities that prove it is derivative.1 The later synthesis of morphological thought, particularly in pedagogical texts, adopted carefully engineered phrasing to accommodate both schools. The standard definition of Tasrif became "تحويل الأصل الواحد إلى أمثلة مختلفة" (transforming the single root into different examples), utilizing the neutral term "الأصل الواحد" (the single root) rather than specifying the Masdar or the Madi.1
Consequently, within any taxonomic classification dealing with verbal morphology, the past tense (Madi) occupies a unique transitional space. It is viewed simultaneously as the foundational bedrock for further temporal inflection (present and imperative) and as the primary canvas for horizontal augmentation (the forms and patterns).
3. Deconstructing the Terminology: The Modern Misconception
A pervasive modern misconception in Arabic morphology—one that directly prompts the user's inquiry—is the assumption that the phrase "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" (deriving verbs from one another) primarily refers to moving across the horizontal axis of verb patterns. Modern students and some contemporary curricula often assume this refers to deriving Form II (فَعَّلَ) or Form X (اسْتَفْعَلَ) from the base Form I (فَعَلَ).
An exhaustive review of the classical source material demonstrates that this assumption is fundamentally incorrect. In standard classical morphological texts, this exact phrase refers specifically, rigorously, and exclusively to temporal derivation—the mechanics of deriving the Present tense (Mudari‘) from the Past tense (Madi), and the Imperative (Amr) from the Present tense.
3.1 Evidence from Shadha al-Arf fi Fann al-Sarf
The definitive modern-classical synthesis text, Shadha al-Arf by Sheikh Ahmad al-Hamlawi, provides the most conclusive and irrefutable evidence regarding the classical usage of this terminology. Al-Hamlawi meticulously structures the book using a series of specific divisions (Taqsimat) of the verb.6
Crucially, he isolates the horizontal and vertical axes. After exhaustively detailing the patterns and augmentations of verbs in earlier sections, he introduces a specific, standalone chapter titled explicitly: "فصل في تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" (Chapter on deriving verbs from one another).6
Within this precisely named chapter, the text makes no mention whatsoever of the Abwab (Forms I-X). It does not discuss how to turn a base verb into a causative verb. Instead, the chapter strictly details the mechanistic phonological rules of generating the three tenses.7 It is divided entirely into operational algorithms governing temporal shifts, explicitly proving that classical scholars equated "deriving verbs from one another" directly with the internal mechanics of Tense (Azmina).
3.2 Distinguishing the Horizontal Axis
If the phrase "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" applies strictly to the vertical mechanics of tense, how do classical texts refer to the horizontal derivation of Form I into Forms II, III, or X?
This horizontal axis is addressed under completely separate, dedicated chapters typically titled "أبواب الفعل" (Verb Doors/Chapters), "المجرد والمزيد" (The Bare and the Augmented), or "معاني صيغ الزوائد" (Meanings of the Augmented Templates).10
Therefore, any taxonomic system must recognize that Abwab and Tasrif al-Af‘al ba‘diha min ba‘d are not synonyms for derivation; rather, the former dictates the lexical semantics and valency of the verb, while the latter dictates the temporal mapping and aspectual mechanics. They represent entirely orthogonal variables in the morphological matrix.
4. The Horizontal Axis: Derivational Morphology and the Abwab
To fully grasp why the Abwab represent a distinct taxonomic category from the Azmina, one must analyze the scope and function of horizontal derivation. In modern linguistic typology, this is classified as Derivational Morphology, which concerns the creation of entirely new lexical items.12 In Arabic, augmenting the bare triliteral root (e.g., ك-ت-ب) with specific patterns or affixes creates new verbs with fundamentally altered semantic implications.
4.1 The Bare Triliteral Forms (المجرد الثلاثي)
The foundation of the horizontal axis is the classification of the bare triliteral verb (Al-Thulathi al-Mujarrad). Classical scholars, through exhaustive induction of the Arabic lexicon, established that triliteral verbs fall into six distinct categories, referred to as Abwab. These doors are identified exclusively by the cross-referencing of the vowel on the middle root letter (‘Ayn al-Kalima) in the past tense with its corresponding vowel in the present tense.10
The classical formulation organizes these six doors based on frequency of use and phonological naturalness.

4.2 The Augmented Forms (المزيد)
The horizontal axis expands massively when accounting for the augmented verbs (Al-Mazid). Verbs can be augmented by one, two, or three letters, reaching a maximum of six letters.15 Each augmentation creates a new Bab that carries specific semantic shifts.
* Augmentation by One Letter: Includes Form II (فَعَّلَ - for intensification or causation), Form III (فَاعَلَ - for associative action or participation), and Form IV (أَفْعَلَ - for converting an intransitive verb to transitive).18
* Augmentation by Two Letters: Includes Form V (تَفَعَّلَ - the reflexive of Form II), Form VI (تَفَاعَلَ - the reflexive of Form III, denoting mutual action), Form VII (انْفَعَلَ - strict mutawa'a or reflexivity), Form VIII (افْتَعَلَ - participation or reflexivity), and Form IX (افْعَلَّ - denoting colors or physical defects).20
* Augmentation by Three Letters: The most prominent is Form X (اسْتَفْعَلَ), which typically denotes seeking or requesting the action (e.g., istakhraja - he sought to extract), or transformation.22 Other rare forms include if‘aw‘ala (افْعَوْعَلَ), if‘awwala (افْعَوَّلَ), and if‘alla (افْعَالَّ).22
This horizontal axis dictates the valency, voice, and semantic intent of the verb. It is a system of generating new vocabulary, which is why dictionaries are organized around these patterns.
5. The Vertical Axis: Inflectional Morphology and Azminat al-Fi'l
In stark contrast to the horizontal generation of new meanings, the vertical axis concerns Inflectional Morphology. This is the domain of Azminat al-Fi‘l (Verb Tenses). Inflectional morphology modifies a word to express grammatical categories such as tense, aspect, mood, person, number, and gender, strictly without creating a new lexical item.12
When taking the Form X verb اسْتَخْرَجَ (he extracted), its transition into يَسْتَخْرِجُ (he extracts) and اسْتَخْرِجْ (extract!) does not alter the fundamental semantic core of "extraction".24 It merely shifts the action across the temporal continuum.

	5.2 The Syntactic Elasticity of Tense
It is critical to note that in classical linguistic frameworks, morphology (Sarf) constantly intersects with syntax (Nahw) regarding the reality of tense. The morphological form of the Madi does not strictly and immutably correlate with past time in semantic reality.
For instance, in conditional clauses utilizing particles such as In (إن) or Law (لو), a morphologically past verb functions temporally in the future or conditional irrealis.29 Furthermore, the past tense form is frequently used in Arabic to express performative utterances, prayers, and supplications (e.g., Salla Allahu 'Alayhi Wa Sallam), where the semantic intent is future or timeless.29
Because of this profound semantic elasticity, grouping "Tense" uniquely under its own hierarchical heading—entirely separate from the rigid structural Abwab—allows taxonomists to cross-link morphological form with syntactic function. It acknowledges that tense is a fluid inflection, not a locked lexical state.
6. The Mechanics of Temporal Derivation (Tasrif al-Af'al Ba'diha Min Ba'd)
To decisively prove that the classical topic of "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" belongs entirely to the vertical axis of tense rather than the horizontal axis of Abwab, one must examine the extreme density and granularity of the rules contained within this framework. The conversion of a past tense verb to a present or imperative is not merely a matter of suffixation; it involves a highly systematic series of morphophonological operations that demand dedicated taxonomic space.
6.1 Deriving the Present from the Past (الماضي إلى المضارع)
As documented explicitly in the dedicated chapter in Shadha al-Arf, generating the Mudari‘ demands strict adherence to sequential constraints 6:
1. Prefix Addition: The grammarian must append one of the Anaytu (أ، ن، ي، ت) letters, known as Ahruf al-Mudara‘a, to the beginning of the past tense base.6
2. Prefix Vocalization (Harakat Harf al-Mudara'a): The vowel applied to this prefix is strictly determined by the character count of the past verb.
   * If the past verb consists of exactly four letters—whether it is a bare quadriliteral (دَحْرَجَ) or an augmented triliteral (أَكْرَمَ, كَبَّرَ)—the prefix must be voweled with a Damma (يُدَحْرِجُ, يُكْرِمُ, يُكَبِّرُ).6
   * Conversely, if the past verb consists of three, five, or six letters, the prefix is universally voweled with a Fatha (يَضْرِبُ, يَنْطَلِقُ, يَسْتَغْفِرُ).6
3. Vocalic Shift of the Penultimate Radical: The vowel preceding the final letter is also subject to transformation.
   * For augmented verbs beginning with an extra Ta' (e.g., تَشَارَكَ, تَعَلَّمَ, تَدَحْرَجَ), the penultimate vowel remains a Fatha (يَتَشَارَكُ, يَتَعَلَّمُ, يَتَدَحْرَجُ).7
   * For all other non-triliteral verbs, the penultimate letter is forcibly shifted to a Kasra (يُعَظِّمُ, يُقَاتِلُ).7
   * For triliteral verbs, the first root letter (Fa' al-Kalima) becomes silent, and the middle letter ('Ayn al-Kalima) takes a Damma, Fatha, or Kasra strictly according to the lexical dictates of the six Abwab (as detailed in Section 4.1).7
4. Consonant Elision: A specific morphophonological rule dictates that the extra Hamza found in Form IV (أَفْعَلَ) is systematically deleted across the entire present tense paradigm. Thus, أَكْرَمَ becomes يُكْرِمُ, effectively avoiding the phonetically cumbersome يُأَكْرِمُ.6
6.2 Deriving the Imperative from the Present (المضارع إلى الأمر)
The derivation of the Imperative (Amr) is procedurally and philosophically dependent on the Mudari‘, not the Madi. The mechanical steps are precise and sequential:
1. Truncation: The foundational step is the removal of the present tense prefix (Harf al-Mudara‘a) from the beginning of the verb (e.g., يُعَظِّمُ naturally becomes عَظِّمْ, and يَتَشَارَكُ becomes تَشَارَكْ).6
2. Epenthesis and the Connective Hamza (Hamzat al-Wasl): A fundamental phonetic law of Arabic prohibits initiating an utterance with a silent consonant (Sukun). Therefore, if the truncation of the prefix exposes a silent consonant, a prosthetic vowel mechanism, the Hamzat Wasl, must be prepended.5
   * For example, removing the prefix from يَسْتَغْفِرُ leaves the silent Sin (سْتَغْفِرْ). To vocalize this, the Hamza is added, yielding اسْتَغْفِرْ.6
3. Vocalization of the Hamzat al-Wasl: The inserted Hamza is not statically voweled. As deeply analyzed in texts like Ibn al-Hajib's Shafiya, the vowel of the Hamza depends on the internal structure of the verb. If the middle root letter ('Ayn al-Kalima) possesses an original Damma, the Hamza takes a Damma (e.g., يَكْتُبُ -> اكْتُبْ, يَغْزُو -> اغْزُ).5 In all other cases—whether the middle letter has a Fatha or a Kasra—the Hamza is voweled with a Kasra (e.g., يَضْرِبُ -> اضْرِبْ, يَفْتَحُ -> افْتَحْ).5
This extensive, algorithmic rule set dictates the flow of time within a single lexical item. It requires a dedicated pedagogical and taxonomic space. Conflating this deeply phonological, rule-based temporal inflection with the broader lexical categorizations of the Abwab would severely compromise the integrity of any morphological database.
7. Comparative Structural Analysis of Classical Treatises
To conclusively answer the prompt's inquiry regarding how these subjects are treated in classical texts—whether as a single unified topic or as distinctly separate domains—we must perform a structural analysis of the table of contents and pedagogical logic of the most authoritative morphological treatises.
7.1 Al-Shafiya by Ibn al-Hajib
Ibn al-Hajib’s Al-Shafiya stands as the undisputed magnum opus of Arabic morphology, serving as the definitive reference for all subsequent grammarians.31 A review of its internal structure reveals a highly nuanced, rigorous separation of the horizontal and vertical axes.
Ibn al-Hajib first defines the architecture of the word (Bina' al-Kalima) and its structural weight (Wazn).33 He addresses the past tense explicitly as the foundational base under chapters titled "أبنية الفعل الماضي المجرد الثلاثي" (Structures of the Bare Triliteral Past Verb) and "أبنية الفعل الماضي الثلاثي المزيد فيه" (Structures of the Augmented Triliteral Past Verb).33
He then dedicates extensive chapters to analyzing the horizontal augmented forms purely through the lens of semantics and valency. These chapters are titled by their function: "معاني صيغة أفعل" (Meanings of the Form Af‘ala), "معاني فاعل" (Meanings of Fa‘ala), "معاني تفاعل" (Meanings of Tafa‘ala), and "معاني صيغة استفعل" (Meanings of the Form Istaf‘ala).33
Only after completely exhausting the lexical semantics of the horizontal augmentations does Ibn al-Hajib transition to the vertical axis of inflection. He initiates this with a distinct chapter titled "المضارع وأبوابه" (The Present Tense and its Doors), explicitly mapping the shift from the static past base into the dynamic temporal flow of the present.33 Thus, Ibn al-Hajib treats the structural templates (Abniyah/Abwab) and the temporal inflection (Azmina) as entirely distinct, sequential topics.
7.2 Shadha al-Arf fi Fann al-Sarf by Al-Hamlawi
Ahmad al-Hamlawi’s Shadha al-Arf provides the clearest blueprint for modern taxonomy by utilizing explicit categorizations.
His التقسيم الثالث للفعل: بحسب التجرد والزيادة (The Third Division of the Verb: According to Bareness and Augmentation) covers the entirety of the horizontal axis. Within this vast section, he details the six doors of the triliteral verb, the augmented forms, and the complex mechanics of how one form transitions to another to create new meaning.15
Subsequently, after defining whether verbs are morphologically locked or fluid (التقسيم الرابع للفعل بحسب الجمود والتصريف), he deploys the pivotal chapter: فصل في تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض (Chapter on Deriving Verbs from One Another).6 As established, this chapter handles solely the mechanical algorithms of moving from Past to Present to Imperative.7 Al-Hamlawi’s structural framework proves that Abwab are treated as an inventory of lexical templates, whereas Tasrif al-Af‘al is treated as an operational algorithm for temporal rendering.
7.3 Matn al-Bina fi al-Sarf and Marah al-Arwah
Matn al-Bina, heavily utilized in intermediate and foundational curricula (such as at Dar al-Hadith al-Khayriyya), takes a uniquely blended approach tailored for memorization rather than deep ontological classification.35 It declares at the outset that morphology comprises exactly 35 Abwab.35 It lists each form by its template, exhaustively covering augmented forms like Fa‘lala, If‘anlala, and Tafaw‘ala.35 It does not isolate tense into a separate philosophical chapter; rather, the tenses are baked directly into the definition of the Abwab as a pedagogical tool.36
Similarly, texts like Marah al-Arwah divide the book by root typologies (The Seven Divisions: Sound, Doubled, Hamzated, Weak, Hollow, Defective, and Wrapped), but within those typologies, they utilize specific chapters for the Past, Future/Present, and Imperative.37

	8. The Intersection of Root Typology and Tense Derivation
To fully appreciate the complexity of the vertical axis and why it requires its own taxonomy, we must analyze how the mechanical derivation of tense intersects with root typologies. Morphology does not exist in a vacuum; the health of the foundational consonants dictates the severity of the structural transformation.
Classical scholars categorize all Arabic roots into "The Seven Divisions" (Al-Aqsam al-Sab‘a): Sound (Salim), Hamzated (Mahmuz), Doubled (Muda‘af), Assimilated (Mithal), Hollow (Ajwaf), Defective (Naqis), and Wrapped (Lafif).35
When applying the rules of "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" (Tense Derivation) to these varying typologies, the inflectional morphology becomes highly volatile.
* The Hollow Verb (Ajwaf): In a sound verb like Nasara, the derivation to the present tense is clean (Yansuru). In a hollow verb like Qala (قَالَ), the temporal derivation to the present tense requires a complex morphophonemic shift to Yaqulu (يَقُولُ), and the derivation to the imperative requires the total deletion of the weak middle letter to prevent a consonant cluster, resulting in Qul (قُلْ).3
* The Defective Verb (Naqis): In verbs ending in a weak letter, such as Rama (رَمَى), the derivation to the imperative requires the deletion of the final weak letter as a sign of grammatical mood, resulting in Irmi (ارْمِ).5
* The Hamzated Verb (Mahmuz): Verbs beginning with a Hamza, such as Akala (أَكَلَ), drop the Hamza entirely in the imperative, resulting in the irregular Kul (كُلْ) rather than the standard template, due to sheer frequency of use and phonetic naturalness.20
These exhaustive, localized phonological rules are the true essence of "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض". They represent the heavy lifting of Arabic morphological inflection. Attempting to subsume these vast paradigms under the category of Abwab (which is focused on semantic meaning and valency) would result in a chaotic, unsearchable ontology.
9. Resolving the Taxonomic Collision: Classification of Scholarly Excerpts
With the theoretical boundaries drawn, we must address the highly practical ontological problem posed by the user: "Could a scholarly excerpt about تصريف الأبواب be cleanly classified under one of these two leaves? Or does it always require both?"
Let us examine a quintessential excerpt detailing the Abwab:
"النوع الثالث: الفعل الثلاثي المزيد بثلاثة أحرف وله أربعة أبواب وهي: استَفْعَلَ يَسْتَفْعِلُ نحو: استَخْرَجَ يَسْتَخْرِجُ..." (The third type: the triliteral verb augmented by three letters, which has four doors, namely: istaf‘ala yastaf‘ilu, for example: istakhraja yastakhriju...).22
At first glance, this excerpt appears to straddle both axes. It explicitly discusses horizontal augmentation ("المزيد بثلاثة أحرف") and directly references the Abwab. Simultaneously, it lists both the Past tense (استَفْعَلَ) and the Present tense (يَسْتَفْعِلُ), deploying the vertical axis of tense. Does this excerpt require cross-tagging under both Abwab and Azmina?
Despite the appearance of the present tense, an expert morphological ontologist must classify this excerpt exclusively under the horizontal axis (i.e., Abwab al-Fi‘l / Al-Mujarrad wa al-Mazid).
The reasoning is rooted in the concept of the "morphological signature." In Arabic lexicography and morphological indexing, a Bab (Form) cannot be identified solely by its past tense. Because vowels fluctuate internally (e.g., the difference between daraba yadrIbu and kataba yaktUbu), the definitive identity of the verb form is inextricably tied to the vowel transition between the Madi and the Mudari‘.
When a classical scholar writes "استَفْعَلَ يَسْتَفْعِلُ", they are not attempting to teach the concept of the present tense. They are not explaining the rules of adding the prefix ya-, nor are they discussing the future aspect. They are merely citing the principal parts of the verb to define its form signature and establish its lexical identity. The present tense here is used diagnostically, not inflectionally.
Therefore, such an excerpt does not require both taxonomic leaves. It belongs cleanly and solely to the Abwab category. A text should only be classified under the Azmina (Tenses) or Tasrif al-Af‘al ba‘diha min ba‘d leaf if it explicitly discusses the morphological rules of temporal conversion—such as the deletion of the Hamzat Wasl from the present tense of Forms VII, VIII, and X, the vocalization of the present tense prefix (Harf al-Mudara‘a) with a Fatha versus a Damma, or the syntactic indicators of the past tense.7
10. Modern Linguistic Typology and Computational Implications
The distinction between these two topics is not merely a matter of historical pedantry; it has profound implications for modern computational linguistics, Natural Language Processing (NLP), and the development of Arabic Treebanks and morphological parsers.39
In modern linguistic typology, the horizontal axis (Abwab) directly maps to Derivational Morphology, while the vertical axis (Azmina) maps to Inflectional Morphology.12
10.1 Corpus Tagging and Parsing Algorithms
When a computer-based morphological parser encounters an Arabic verb, it must break the token down into its constituent features. If the parser encounters the token yastakhrijuna (يَسْتَخْرِجُونَ - they extract), it must assign morphological tags.
* The Lexical Tag (Derivational): The parser identifies the root (خ-ر-ج) and the pattern template (استفعل). This determines the lexical meaning (to extract). This is the digital equivalent of identifying the Bab.
* The Feature Tag (Inflectional): The parser then identifies the tense (Imperfective/Present), the person (Third), the number (Plural), and the gender (Masculine). This is the digital equivalent of identifying the Azmina and the application of Tasrif al-Af'al ba'diha min ba'd.
If an ontology or database merges these two concepts, creating parsing rules becomes computationally impossible. An algorithm must know that moving from Form I to Form X requires a new dictionary lookup (because the meaning changes), but moving from Past to Present merely requires updating the temporal tag while retaining the same dictionary definition.19
10.2 Sociolinguistic Shifts
Furthermore, modern comparative studies between Classical Arabic, Modern Standard Arabic (MSA), and colloquial spoken dialects heavily rely on this distinction.40 Dialects frequently simplify inflectional morphology (e.g., losing the dual number or the feminine plural in verb conjugations, altering the present tense prefixes as seen in Maghrebi Arabic).40 However, the derivational morphology—the core Abwab—remains robustly intact across almost all dialects, as the need to express causation, reflexivity, and intensity is universal.42 This differing rate of linguistic evolution further demands that the two axes remain taxonomically separated.
11. Strategic Taxonomic Recommendations
Having exhaustively dissected the classical epistemological frameworks, the lexicographical structures, the mechanistic rules of derivation, and the modern computational implications, we can definitively answer the core inquiry: Are these two topics genuinely distinct?
Yes, they are fundamentally distinct. They represent orthogonal dimensions of morphological analysis.
* أبواب الأفعال / المجرد والمزيد: Operates strictly on the horizontal axis. It generates new dictionary entries, alters verbal valency (transitivity), and creates semantic meaning (causation, reciprocity, mutawa'a).
* أزمنة الفعل / تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض: Operates strictly on the vertical axis. It provides the algorithmic phonological rules for moving a single dictionary entry across the continuum of time. It is crucial to reiterate that authoritative texts like Shadha al-Arf use the phrase "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" synonymously with the derivation of Tense, not the expansion of Forms.
11.1 The Recommended Taxonomic Restructuring
Merging these two subjects into a single leaf would represent a severe taxonomic regression, conflating derivational morphology with inflectional morphology and violating the structural logic established by titans of the field such as Ibn al-Hajib and Al-Hamlawi.
However, keeping them as entirely isolated, unconnected top-level leaves ignores the reality that they both fall squarely under the umbrella of "Verbal Morphology" (Sarf al-Af‘al).
Therefore, the optimal structural recommendation is to implement a Parent/Children Hierarchical Architecture. This model accurately reflects the linguistic domain, accommodates excerpts that reference both axes, and honors the classical Arabic epistemological structure.

	The derivation of the passive form (e.g., fu‘ila yuf‘alu) from the active form. Like tense, voice is an inflectional, not a derivational, modification.10
	12. Conclusion
The rigorous structuring of Arabic morphological knowledge is not merely an exercise in archival sorting; it reflects the deep, underlying mathematical reality of the Arabic language. The taxonomic dilemma regarding whether to separate or merge the derivation of verb forms and the derivation of verb tenses strikes at the heart of an operational ambiguity that has confused modern students and database architects alike: the erroneous conflation of the classical phrase "تصريف الأفعال بعضها من بعض" with the derivation of the augmented verb forms (Abwab).
As proven unequivocally by the structural mapping of classical texts like Shadha al-Arf fi Fann al-Sarf and Al-Shafiya, this specific phrase was historically deployed to teach the mechanical, phonological generation of the Present and Imperative tenses from the Past tense—not the semantic transition from Form I to Form II or Form X.
Consequently, "الأبواب" and "الأزمنة" represent fundamentally distinct morphological phenomena that must not be merged. The former dictates the semantic architecture, valency, and dictionary identity of a verb through derivational morphology. The latter dictates its temporal state, aspect, and syntactic interaction through inflectional morphology.
By implementing the recommended Parent/Child taxonomic hierarchy, researchers, curriculum developers, and computational linguists can achieve a highly precise, classically authentic, and technologically sound classification system. This structure will ensure that the complex lexical signatures of the Abwab are not cluttered by the algorithmic rules of temporal inflection, thereby mirroring the true operational majesty and precision of ‘Ilm al-Sarf.
