# KR Taxonomy Tree Protocol — Science Tree Validation

**Authority:** This protocol governs ALL taxonomy tree validation. It is mandatory before any tree is committed to `library/sciences/{science}/tree.yaml` as an active version. No tree reaches the pipeline without passing every gate defined here.

**Governing principle:** `reference/protocols/QUALITY_AXIOM.md` — The architect is the sole quality gate. The owner does not validate, does not make decisions, and does not review output. Every error the architect makes reaches the pipeline.

**Knowledge Integrity Axiom:** Every leaf in a taxonomy tree becomes an encyclopedic entry in the owner's study library. Every wrong node — a fabricated topic, a misplaced concept, a missing real topic — becomes a WRONG BELIEF in the owner's mind. The damage is silent: no crash, no error message. The owner is a beginning Islamic studies student who trusts the tree completely. If a leaf is wrong, the owner builds a wrong mental model of how the science is organized. There are no "low-severity" tree errors.

**Failure history:**
- **Pre-protocol (all 5 sciences):** All v1_0 trees were generated by AI without documented methodology, without independent verification, without corpus validation, and without owner review. They were annotated as "placement-safe" (structurally compatible with the engine) but NOT as "scholarly valid." The nahw v1_0 tree (226 leaves, 12 branches) was found to have no traceable provenance. Gate G3 was defined in ENGINE_FACTORY_PLAN.md but never executed.
- **Nahw pilot — convergence trap:** The original synthesis methodology said "don't second-guess convergent decisions." This was dangerous: two independent syntheses drawing from the same 4 researcher inputs can converge on the same error. Fixed in prompt hardening (commit `9f6a7432`): convergence is evidence, not proof.
- **Nahw pilot — tail-end quality:** The comparison and review prompts for the merge process were written at the end of a long synthesis session. Per the Quality Axiom, tail-end output is the lowest quality. Both prompts required significant hardening in a fresh session (10 improvements each).
- **Nahw pilot — gold baseline provenance:** The gold baseline was assigned by the architect, not a domain expert. Its validity for evaluating the tree is uncertain.

## 1. Purpose and Scope

### What this protocol does

Defines the complete process for validating a taxonomy tree — from commissioning independent research through multi-provider merge to final commit. It transforms an unvalidated AI-generated tree into a validated, evidence-backed tree with documented provenance for every node.

### When to invoke

Invoke this protocol when:
- A science tree needs initial validation (Gate G3 for any science)
- An existing validated tree needs major revision (adding/removing branches, not just leaf adjustments)
- A new science is added to the library

### What it produces

A validated `library/sciences/{science}/tree.yaml` with:
- Documented methodology and researcher provenance
- Per-leaf confidence ratings backed by cross-stream evidence
- Complete audit trail (researcher outputs, synthesis artifacts, review verdicts)
- Updated registry entry in `library/sciences/taxonomy_registry.yaml`
- Compatibility-tested against the taxonomy engine loader

### What it does NOT cover

- Minor leaf adjustments (adding 1–2 leaves based on pipeline feedback) — handle via normal CC handoff
- Gold baseline regeneration — that is a downstream task triggered by tree validation
- LLM placement prompt tuning — that is taxonomy Session 2+ work

## 2. Prerequisites

Before starting the validation process, the following must exist:

### 2.1 Corpus data

The science must have corpus-based research from the owner's Shamela collection. At minimum:
- **Heading extraction** from the identified books for this science (equivalent to `codex_nahw_corpus_tree.yaml`)
- **Topic frequency data** showing how many books discuss each topic (equivalent to `codex_nahw_topic_frequency.json`)
- **Corpus gap analysis** listing topics that appear in N+ books but are not in any proposed tree (equivalent to `codex_nahw_corpus_gaps.md`)
- **Content analysis** of the 2–3 largest books showing sub-topic granularity within chapters (equivalent to `codex_nahw_content_analysis.md`)

If corpus data does not exist, commission it BEFORE starting the tree validation. The corpus is the ground truth for "what topics exist" — without it, the tree is pure speculation.

### 2.2 Existing tree (if any)

If an unvalidated v1_0 tree exists at `library/sciences/{science}/tree.yaml`, note its leaf count and structure but DO NOT read it until Step 9 of the synthesis (anti-anchoring rule). Reading the existing tree early biases the synthesis toward reproducing its structure rather than deriving the correct structure from evidence.

### 2.3 Science boundary definitions

Identify which OTHER sciences share boundary topics with this science. For every boundary pair:
- Define the principle that separates them (e.g., nahw = sentence-level syntax, sarf = word-level morphology)
- List specific topics that fall on the boundary with a ruling for each
- Document the ruling source (canonical textbook organization, scholarly consensus, pragmatic decision with reasoning)

See §6 for the nahw/sarf boundary as a worked example.

### 2.4 Architect readiness

The architect session must:
- Clone the repo fresh (never work from stale memory)
- Read `reference/protocols/QUALITY_AXIOM.md`
- Read `reference/GOVERNANCE.md`
- Invoke skills: `critical-review`, `thinking-frameworks`, `kr-research`, `prompt-engineer`
- Confirm the cross-provider review team will be invoked (this is a major decision)

## 3. The 4-Researcher Methodology

### 3.1 Principle

Commission 4 independent researchers — 2 knowledge-based and 2 corpus-based — to build trees from different evidence sources. None see the others' work. This prevents groupthink and ensures the final tree reflects multiple valid perspectives.

The knowledge-based researchers provide scholarly organization (how SHOULD topics be grouped). The corpus-based researchers provide empirical reality (what topics ACTUALLY exist in the owner's book collection). The synthesis merges both: organization from knowledge, existence from corpus.

### 3.2 Knowledge-based researchers (2)

**Researcher K-1: ChatGPT Pro (Deep Research mode)**

Input specification:
- The science name and a 2–3 sentence scope definition (e.g., "علم النحو: Arabic syntax, covering how words relate to each other in sentences. Excludes morphology/sarf.")
- A list of 3–5 canonical textbooks for this science as starting points
- The target leaf range (150–250 for major sciences, 50–150 for minor sciences)
- Instruction to build a tree from canonical texts, web research, and scholarly encyclopedias

Output requirements:
- Complete YAML tree with Arabic titles and Latin IDs
- A justification table: for each Level-1 branch and Level-2 topic, which canonical source(s) organize the science this way
- Leaf count per branch

Prompt template: Base on `reference/research/chatgpt_synthesis_prompt.md`, replacing science-specific details.

**Researcher K-2: Fresh Claude Opus (Research mode)**

Input specification: Same as K-1, but given in a SEPARATE fresh chat with zero context from K-1. The two knowledge researchers must be completely independent.

Output requirements: Same as K-1.

### 3.3 Corpus-based researchers (2)

**Researcher C-1: CC or Codex CLI (heading extraction)**

Input specification:
- The list of books in the owner's Shamela collection that belong to this science (from the source engine's metadata or a manual selection)
- Instruction to extract all chapter headings, count topic frequency, and build a flat topic tree

Output requirements:
- A corpus tree YAML (flat structure — no deep hierarchy needed)
- A topic frequency JSON: `{topic_arabic: book_count}`
- A corpus gaps list: topics in 5+ books not captured by the corpus tree
- A books-identified list: which books were analyzed

**Researcher C-2: CC or Codex CLI (content analysis)**

Input specification:
- The 2–3 largest books from C-1's analysis
- Instruction to analyze chapter structure in depth: how many distinct sub-topics exist within each chapter?

Output requirements:
- A content analysis document showing sub-topics per chapter
- Summary statistics: average sub-topics per chapter, maximum possible leaf count
- The chapter ordering of the largest book (often reflects canonical pedagogical sequence)

### 3.4 Independence rules

- No researcher sees another's output before completing their own
- The architect does not share preliminary conclusions with any researcher
- If a researcher asks for guidance (e.g., "should I include sarf?"), provide only the science boundary definition from §6, not other researchers' decisions
- All 4 outputs are committed to `reference/research/` before synthesis begins

## 4. Cross-Stream Scoring Matrix

When synthesizing the 4 researcher outputs, every proposed topic receives a confidence score based on which streams (knowledge vs. corpus) support it.

### 4.1 Scoring rules

| Knowledge support | Corpus support | Confidence | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Both K-1 and K-2 include | C-1 or C-2 confirms | **HIGH** | Include without debate |
| Both K-1 and K-2 include | No corpus evidence | **MEDIUM-HIGH** | Include (scholarly consensus) |
| One knowledge researcher | C-1 or C-2 confirms | **HIGH** | Include (corpus validates) |
| One knowledge researcher | No corpus evidence | **MEDIUM** | Flag for investigation |
| Neither knowledge researcher | C-1 or C-2 confirms | **MEDIUM** | Include (real topic in books) |
| Single researcher only | No other support | **LOW** | Investigate before including |

### 4.2 Interpretation

- **HIGH** means the topic is supported by both scholarly knowledge AND empirical corpus evidence. These are the backbone of the tree.
- **MEDIUM-HIGH** means scholars agree but the corpus didn't capture it. This usually means the topic is discussed within chapters rather than as a standalone heading.
- **MEDIUM** means partial support. Investigate: is the topic real but under a different name? Is it a subtopic of something else? Is it a boundary case with another science?
- **LOW** means only one source proposes this. Do NOT include without investigation. It may be: (a) fabricated by an LLM, (b) a boundary topic belonging to another science, (c) a real but very rare topic, or (d) a topic under a non-standard name.

### 4.3 Critical warning: convergence ≠ correctness

Two independent syntheses that draw from the same 4 researcher inputs can converge on the same error. If all 4 researchers share a misconception (e.g., all include a sarf topic because it appears in nahw textbooks alongside nahw content), convergence is HIGH but the node is still wrong.

**Mitigation:** For every HIGH-confidence node, the corpus data (`topic_frequency.json`) serves as an independent check. If corpus frequency is zero for a topic that both knowledge researchers include, flag it — the topic may not actually exist as a standalone concept in the owner's book collection.

## 5. Synthesis Steps

The synthesis transforms 4 researcher outputs into 1 validated tree. It MUST be done step by step, one step per response, with the owner saying "continue" between steps.

**Why step-by-step:** A single-pass synthesis produces surface-level analysis. Steps 1–4 build understanding. Steps 5–8 build structure. Steps 9–11 verify. Skipping ahead produces a tree shaped by first impressions rather than evidence.

**Minimum depth:** Each step should be a substantive analysis. If a step takes fewer than ~400 words, the architect is going too fast and missing nuance.

### Step 1: Inventory all researcher outputs

Read all 4 tree files and the content analysis. Do NOT form conclusions yet. Inventory:
- Each researcher's Level-1 branches (list side by side)
- Each researcher's leaf count
- Each researcher's organizational principle
- Any obvious gaps or surprises

### Step 2: Branch-level alignment

Map Level-1 branches across all 4 researchers:
- Which branches represent the SAME conceptual division despite different names?
- Which branches appear in only one researcher's output?
- Does corpus evidence suggest one organization over another?

Apply `thinking-frameworks`: what are the competing organizational principles? Which serves an encyclopedic reference best? What's the strongest argument AGAINST the emerging preference?

Propose the Level-1 structure with reasoning.

### Step 3: Topic-level validation (per branch)

For EACH proposed Level-1 branch:
- List all Level-2 topics from all researchers that belong here
- Note which researchers included each topic
- Cross-reference with corpus frequency data
- Apply the scoring matrix from §4
- Flag any LOW-confidence topics for investigation

This step may span multiple responses if there are many branches.

### Step 4: Leaf-level decisions

For each Level-2 topic:
- What sub-topics do researchers propose as leaves?
- Does the content analysis provide evidence of distinct sub-topics?
- Is each proposed leaf DISTINCT from its siblings?
- Does the leaf represent a topic a student of this science would recognize?

Target leaf count: **150–250 for major sciences** (nahw, sarf, balagha), **50–150 for minor sciences** (aqidah, imlaa). Fewer than the minimum is too coarse for thousands of books. More than 400 is too fine for LLM-based classification.

### Step 5: Corpus gap check

Read the corpus gaps file. Focus on topics appearing in **15+ books**:
- Missing from the tree? → Is it this science or another? Already covered under a different name? A genuine gap?
- Exclude: generic headings (المقدمة, وفاته, مصادر ومراجع), biographical entries, topics belonging to other sciences

### Step 6: Science boundary verification

Apply the boundary rules from §6. Search all proposed titles for boundary-science keywords. For each hit, determine: is this a genuine this-science topic, or has a boundary topic leaked in?

### Step 7: ChatGPT synthesis comparison (if applicable)

If ChatGPT Pro has already completed its parallel synthesis (and its output is available), do an initial comparison against your independent analysis from Steps 1–6. Note points of agreement and disagreement. This is a PRELIMINARY comparison — the formal dual-provider comparison happens in §8 Phase 2, where ChatGPT receives both trees and produces an evidence-based merge.

If ChatGPT's synthesis is not yet available, skip this step. The merge process (§8) handles both cases.

### Step 8: Produce the YAML tree

Build the complete tree in the schema defined in §10. Go branch by branch. Save to `reference/research/{science}_v2_0_draft.yaml` (NOT the final location).

### Step 9: Self-review (adversarial)

Verify with tool calls — not memory:
1. Valid YAML? (parse with Python)
2. All IDs unique? (extract, check duplicates)
3. All IDs in correct format? (lowercase Latin, underscores only)
4. Leaf count in target range?
5. No boundary-science leakage? (keyword scan)
6. Every L1 branch has children? No empty nodes?
7. Granularity uniformity? (leaf count per branch — flag extremes)
8. Sibling distinctness? (for each L2 with 4+ leaves, can you state what distinguishes each?)
9. Scholarly reality check? (pick 10 random leaves, web-verify each)
10. NOW read the existing unvalidated tree. Any concerning topic losses?

### Step 10: Prepare merge process prompts

Adapt the existing merge process prompts for this science (see §8.4 for adaptation rules):
- Base comparison prompt: `reference/research/chatgpt_comparison_prompt.md`
- Base review prompt: `reference/research/fresh_claude_review_prompt.md`

Save adapted versions to `reference/research/`. The architect's synthesis (Steps 1–9) is now Phase 1 of §8. The owner fires the adapted prompts to begin Phases 2 and 3.

### Step 11: Finalize after merge

After the 3-phase merge (§8) completes and the architect runs all quality gates (§9) on the final tree, proceed to the Post-Validation Steps section of this protocol (archive old tree, install new tree, update registry, run regression tests).

## 6. Science Boundary Rules

### 6.1 General principle

Each tree covers ONE Islamic science. Topics that cross boundaries are assigned to one science based on: (a) which science primarily DISCUSSES this topic in canonical textbooks, and (b) pragmatic lookup — where would a student look for it?

The boundary definition must be documented before synthesis begins. It should answer: "If a topic appears in books of BOTH sciences, which tree owns it?"

### 6.2 Worked example: Nahw/Sarf boundary

**Principle:** Nahw covers sentence-level syntax (how words relate to each other in sentences — إعراب, عوامل, تراكيب). Sarf covers word-level morphology (how individual words are formed — أبنية, تصريف, إعلال).

**Sarf topics to EXCLUDE from the nahw tree:**

| Topic | Arabic | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Morphological patterns | التصريف | Core sarf |
| Vowel weakening | الإعلال | Word-internal sound change |
| Letter substitution | الإبدال | Word-internal sound change |
| Letter assimilation | الإدغام | Word-internal sound change |
| Verbal noun patterns | أبنية المصادر | Word formation |
| Noun/verb patterns | أبنية الأسماء والأفعال | Word formation |
| Relative adjective | النسب | Word formation |
| Diminutive | التصغير | Word formation |
| Pausal form | الوقف | Word-final phonology |
| Vowel tilting | الإمالة | Phonological rule |
| Feminization | التأنيث | Word formation |

**Debatable boundary cases (with rulings from nahw pilot):**

| Topic | Nahw pilot ruling | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| جمع التكسير | Excluded (sarf) | Broken plural formation is morphological. The SYNTACTIC behavior of broken plurals (agreement rules) is covered under nahw agreement topics. |
| الممدود والمقصور | Excluded (sarf) | These are word-form categories. Their syntactic behavior (case marking) is handled under the relevant nahw syntactic topics. |

### 6.3 Deriving boundary rules for other science pairs

For each new science, identify overlapping sciences and produce a similar table:

**Balagha/Nahw:** Balagha discusses sentence construction for rhetorical effect (تقديم وتأخير, قصر, فصل ووصل). Nahw discusses the same constructions for grammatical correctness. Rule: if the topic is about WHY a structure has rhetorical force → balagha. If it's about WHAT is grammatically correct → nahw.

**Aqidah/other sciences:** Aqidah is largely self-contained. Potential boundary: قواعد الأسماء والصفات (names and attributes of Allah) touches على أصول الفقه in methodology. Rule: theological content → aqidah. Methodological principles → usul al-fiqh (if it exists as a science in the library).

**Sarf/Imlaa:** Sarf discusses word formation rules. Imlaa discusses how words are written. Overlap on: همزة الوصل والقطع, الألف المقصورة والممدودة. Rule: if the topic is about PRONUNCIATION and FORM → sarf. If it's about ORTHOGRAPHY (writing conventions) → imlaa.

The boundary table for each science pair must be produced and committed to `reference/research/{science}_boundary_rules.md` before synthesis begins.

## 7. Corpus Gap Checking

### 7.1 Purpose

The corpus gap check verifies the tree against empirical reality: what topics ACTUALLY appear in the owner's book collection. A tree that covers every topic in canonical textbooks but misses topics that appear in 30 real books has a real gap.

### 7.2 Process

1. Read the corpus gaps file (`reference/research/{science}_corpus_gaps.md` or equivalent)
2. Focus on topics appearing in **15+ books** — these are definitely real and common
3. For each high-frequency topic not in the tree:
   - Is it this science or another? (Apply boundary rules from §6)
   - Is it a generic heading? (المقدمة, وفاته, مصادر ومراجع, فائدة, شيوخه → exclude)
   - Is it already covered under a different name in the tree?
   - If it's a genuine gap: where should it go?
4. For topics in **5–14 books**: scan for genuine gaps, but don't add a leaf for every low-frequency topic

### 7.3 Frequency thresholds

| Book count | Action |
|---|---|
| 20+ | MUST be in tree (or documented as boundary/generic exclusion) |
| 15–19 | SHOULD be in tree (investigate any absences) |
| 5–14 | MAY be in tree if supported by knowledge researchers |
| < 5 | Do NOT add solely based on frequency (too rare to justify a leaf) |

### 7.4 Corpus data reliability

The corpus data is the best available empirical evidence for which topics exist in the owner's collection, but it is not infallible. Known error sources:
- **Heading extraction errors:** Regex-based heading extraction can misidentify text as headings or miss real headings. Frequency counts may be inflated or deflated.
- **Book misidentification:** A book tagged as one science might actually cover multiple sciences. Sarf chapters in a "nahw" book inflate nahw topic frequencies.
- **Duplicate counting:** If the same book appears under multiple titles, its headings are counted multiple times.

**Mitigation:** The scholarly gates (§9 S-1 through S-6) verify the tree against real-world evidence independently of corpus data. If a topic has high corpus frequency but fails the scholarly reality check (S-1), investigate the corpus data — the frequency may be an artifact. Do not treat corpus frequency as infallible; treat it as the strongest available signal, cross-checked by scholarly verification.

## 8. Multi-Provider Merge Process

### 8.1 Why multi-provider

Session 28 proved that a single reviewer — even a thorough one — misses blocking bugs that a fresh reviewer catches instantly. The taxonomy tree is a MAJOR decision (it shapes every encyclopedic entry). The cross-provider review team (reference/GOVERNANCE.md) is mandatory.

### 8.2 The 3-phase process

**Phase 1: Independent synthesis (Architect — Claude Chat)**

The architect follows Steps 1–9 from §5, producing a draft tree at `reference/research/{science}_v2_0_draft.yaml`. This is committed to the repo with all supporting artifacts (leaf inventory, synthesis notes).

Simultaneously or sequentially, ChatGPT Pro runs its own independent synthesis of the same 4 researcher inputs using a parallel prompt (base: `reference/research/chatgpt_synthesis_prompt.md`, adapted for the science).

**Phase 2: Comparison (ChatGPT Pro)**

ChatGPT Pro receives BOTH trees (its own synthesis + the architect's draft) and a comparison prompt (base: `reference/research/chatgpt_comparison_prompt.md`, adapted for the science). ChatGPT:
- Compares both trees systematically
- Resolves every disagreement with evidence (corpus data, researcher counts, canonical sources)
- Produces a single recommended merged tree

**Why ChatGPT for comparison:** ChatGPT Pro's deep research mode excels at systematic, evidence-heavy comparison tasks. It can hold both trees in context and produce exhaustive side-by-side analysis.

**Phase 3: Adversarial review (Fresh Claude Opus)**

A fresh Claude Opus instance — in a NEW chat with zero prior context — receives the merged tree and a review prompt (base: `reference/research/fresh_claude_review_prompt.md`, adapted for the science). The reviewer:
- Checks structural/mechanical soundness
- Verifies scholarly reality of random leaves via web search
- Checks coverage against corpus frequency data
- Verifies science boundary compliance
- Critiques organizational choices
- Checks sibling distinctness and granularity uniformity
- Delivers a READY/NOT READY verdict

**Why fresh Claude for review:** Zero context eliminates confirmation bias. The reviewer has no investment in any decision and no memory of the reasoning process. Session 28 proved fresh eyes catch what deep-context reviewers miss.

### 8.3 Handling findings

- If Phase 2 produces a clean merge with minor adjustments → proceed to Phase 3
- If Phase 2 reveals fundamental disagreements → architect resolves with evidence, updates the merged tree, then proceeds to Phase 3
- If Phase 3 delivers NOT READY → fix all blocking findings, then re-run Phase 3 with a NEW fresh Claude instance (the original reviewer has lost its "fresh eyes" advantage)
- If Phase 3 delivers READY with no adjustments needed → architect runs ALL quality gates from §9 on the final tree with their own tool calls. Only after all gates PASS → proceed to §11
- If Phase 3 delivers READY with minor concerns → architect decides whether to act on each concern. If the tree is modified:
  - Changes affecting ≤3 leaves (renames, minor adjustments): run §9 quality gates on modified tree. If all PASS → proceed to §11
  - Changes affecting >3 leaves or any structural change (branch reorganization, topic relocation): re-run Phase 3 with a NEW fresh Claude instance on the modified tree

**Why the architect must run §9 gates independently:** Fresh Claude's 8 checks overlap with §9 but are not identical. The architect's quality gates include engine loader testing (M-8) in the architect's own environment, confidence justification verification (S-6), and tool-verified versions of all mechanical gates. Relying solely on Fresh Claude's review is single-point-of-failure — the architect must independently verify.

### 8.4 Prompt adaptation for new sciences

When adapting the base prompts for a new science:
1. Replace all nahw-specific references (sarf boundary keywords, nahw canonical texts, nahw topic examples)
2. Add science-specific boundary keywords from §6
3. Add science-specific canonical textbook names
4. Adjust leaf count targets if the science is smaller/larger than nahw
5. Include any lessons from prior science validations in a "known issues" section

## 9. Quality Gates

### 9.1 Mechanical gates (automated, pass/fail)

All mechanical gates must pass before scholarly review. A failing mechanical gate blocks the process.

| Gate | Check | Pass criteria | Tool |
|---|---|---|---|
| M-1 | YAML validity | Parses without error | `yaml.safe_load()` in Python |
| M-2 | ID uniqueness | Zero duplicate IDs across entire tree | Extract all IDs, check `len(ids) == len(set(ids))` |
| M-3 | ID format | All IDs match `^[a-z][a-z0-9_]*$` | Regex scan |
| M-4 | Leaf marking | Every leaf has `leaf: true`; no non-leaf has `leaf: true` | Script validation |
| M-5 | No empty nodes | Every non-leaf has at least 1 child | Tree traversal |
| M-6 | Leaf count | Within target range for this science | `grep -c "leaf: true"` |
| M-7 | Boundary scan | Zero hits on boundary-science keywords (from §6 table) | `grep` Arabic keywords |
| M-8 | Engine loader | Tree loads successfully in taxonomy engine | `load_tree()` with `override_path` |

### 9.2 Scholarly gates (manual, judgment-based)

These require the architect's judgment and tool-based verification. Each gate must be explicitly checked and documented.

| Gate | Check | Pass criteria |
|---|---|---|
| S-1 | Random leaf reality | Pick 15 leaves by systematic sampling (evenly-spaced indices). Web-verify each as a real topic in this science, discussed in at least one named canonical text. ≤1 failure acceptable; ≥2 failures blocks. |
| S-2 | Sibling distinctness | For every L2 topic with 4+ leaves: each leaf can be distinguished from its siblings in one sentence. Zero overlapping pairs. |
| S-3 | Granularity uniformity | No L1 branch has >3× the leaves of another L1 branch of comparable scholarly importance. No L2 topic has >8 leaves without sub-topic splitting. No single-child L2 topics (merge into parent or expand). |
| S-4 | Top-frequency coverage | All topics appearing in 20+ books from the corpus frequency data are either (a) in the tree, (b) documented as a boundary exclusion, or (c) documented as a generic heading exclusion. Zero undocumented absences. |
| S-5 | Organizational coherence | Every L1 branch covers a coherent, non-overlapping area. No "grab-bag" branches. A student of this science would expect to find each topic where it is placed. |
| S-6 | Confidence justification | Every MEDIUM and LOW confidence leaf has documented reasoning. No confidence rating assigned without evidence. |

### 9.3 Verdict

Two verdicts only: **PASS** (all gates clear) or **BLOCKED** (any gate fails).

If BLOCKED: fix all failing gates, then re-run ALL gates (not just the failing ones — fixes can introduce new failures).

## 10. YAML Schema

### 10.1 Required structure

```yaml
taxonomy:
  id: {science}_v{major}_{minor}        # e.g., nahw_v2_0
  title: العنوان العربي للعلم              # e.g., علم النحو
  methodology: "description of how this tree was produced"
  validated: true                         # false until protocol completes
  date: YYYY-MM-DD
  researchers:                            # audit trail
    - "Researcher description: N leaves, M branches"
  leaf_count: N                           # total leaves
  nodes:                                  # the tree structure
  - id: branch_id
    title: العنوان العربي
    children:
    - id: topic_id
      title: عنوان الموضوع
      children:
      - id: leaf_id
        title: عنوان الورقة
        leaf: true
        confidence: HIGH
```

### 10.2 Field rules

| Field | Required | Rules |
|---|---|---|
| `id` | Yes (every node) | Lowercase Latin, underscores only. Pattern: `^[a-z][a-z0-9_]*$`. Unique across entire tree. |
| `title` | Yes (every node) | Arabic scholarly terminology as used in canonical texts. |
| `children` | Yes (non-leaf) | List of child nodes. At least 1 child. |
| `leaf` | Yes (leaf only) | Must be `true`. Non-leaf nodes must NOT have this field. |
| `confidence` | Yes (leaf only) | `HIGH`, `MEDIUM`, or `LOW`. Based on cross-stream scoring matrix (§4). |
| `methodology` | Yes (root) | Human-readable description for audit trail. |
| `validated` | Yes (root) | `true` only after full protocol completion. |
| `researchers` | Recommended (root) | List of researcher descriptions for provenance. |
| `leaf_count` | Recommended (root) | Total leaves for quick reference. |

### 10.3 Engine loader compatibility

The taxonomy engine's tree loader (`engines/taxonomy/src/tree_loader.py`) requires:
- Top-level key: `taxonomy` containing `id`, `title`, `nodes`
- Per node: `id` (string), `title` (string), `children` (list) or `leaf` (boolean)
- Extra fields (`confidence`, `methodology`, `researchers`, etc.) are silently ignored by the loader — include them as metadata

Verify with:
```python
from pathlib import Path
from engines.taxonomy.src.tree_loader import load_tree
tree = load_tree(
    science_id="{science}",
    registry_path=Path("library/sciences/taxonomy_registry.yaml"),
    override_path=Path("path/to/draft.yaml"),
)
assert tree.leaf_count > 0
```

### 10.4 Depth convention

Trees use a 3-level hierarchy:
- **Level 1 (L1):** Branches — major divisions of the science (7–12 per tree)
- **Level 2 (L2):** Topics — thematic groupings within a branch (2–8 per branch)
- **Level 3 (L3):** Leaves — specific topics that become encyclopedic entries

Deeper nesting (L4+) is allowed but discouraged. Most LLM-based classification works best with 2–3 levels of hierarchy. If a topic needs L4 leaves, consider whether the L3 node should be promoted to L2 instead.

## 12. Lessons Learned from Nahw Pilot

These specific lessons were learned during the first application of this methodology (nahw tree, March 2026). Future sessions should internalize them rather than rediscovering them.

### 12.1 The anti-anchoring rule saves real quality

Do NOT read the existing unvalidated tree until Step 9. The nahw v1_0 tree (226 leaves, 12 branches, case-based organization) was structurally valid but organizationally problematic for encyclopedic lookup. Reading it early would have biased the synthesis toward reproducing its structure. The synthesis produced a fundamentally different organization (146 leaves, 9 branches, functional grouping) that serves encyclopedic reference better.

### 12.2 Corpus frequency is the strongest signal

The corpus data (302 books, 70,001 headings for nahw) was the single most valuable input. When knowledge researchers disagreed about whether to include a topic, corpus frequency resolved it: if 20+ books discuss it, it's real; if 0 books discuss it as a standalone heading, it may be fabricated or a subtopic of something else.

### 12.3 Content analysis calibrates granularity

The content analysis of the 3 largest books (587 potential leaves at maximum granularity, average 4.52 sub-topics per chapter) provided the upper bound on useful granularity. This data directly informed the target of 150–250 leaves: enough to capture real distinctions, not so many that LLM classification becomes unreliable.

### 12.4 Tail-end work must be reviewed in a fresh session

The comparison and review prompts were written at the end of a long synthesis session. Both required 10 significant improvements when reviewed fresh. Rule: NEVER commit prompts, protocols, or critical documents written in the last 20% of a long session without fresh-session review.

### 12.5 The convergence trap is real

The original instruction "don't second-guess convergent decisions" was dangerous. If two syntheses draw from the same flawed input, they converge on the same error. The fix: treat convergence as strong evidence but verify against corpus data as an independent check.

### 12.6 Boundary topics require explicit rulings

The nahw/sarf boundary required explicit per-topic rulings for 13+ topics. "Use your judgment" is insufficient — different sessions make different judgments. The boundary table (§6.2) ensures consistency across sessions and across sciences.

### 12.7 MEDIUM-confidence leaves need documented reasoning

The nahw draft had 6 MEDIUM-confidence leaves. Without documented reasoning, a future session cannot evaluate whether they should be upgraded to HIGH (more evidence found) or downgraded to removal (insufficient evidence). The leaf inventory (`nahw_v2_leaf_inventory.md`) with per-leaf reasoning is essential — require it for every science.

### 12.8 The 4 researcher outputs have different failure modes

- **Knowledge researchers** (ChatGPT, Claude): tend to produce well-organized trees with fabricated or overly-fine distinctions. Their trees LOOK scholarly but may include divisions no grammarian has ever made.
- **Corpus researchers** (CC, Codex): tend to produce flat, literal trees that mirror book chapter headings. Their trees are empirically grounded but poorly organized and may miss topics that books discuss within chapters rather than as headings.

The synthesis must leverage the strengths of each: use knowledge researchers for ORGANIZATION and corpus researchers for EXISTENCE verification.

### 12.9 Single-child nodes are a smell

If an L2 topic has exactly 1 leaf, the leaf and the L2 topic are redundant — the leaf IS the topic. Either expand the L2 with more leaves or merge the L2 into its parent branch. The nahw synthesis found this pattern and resolved it in Step 4.

### 12.10 The owner cannot validate but CAN signal

The owner has no technical background and does not make decisions. But the owner's intuitions about whether the tree "feels right" for how he has encountered the science are a useful late-stage signal. The owner review gate (post-Phase 3) is the only point where domain experience enters the process. It is valuable but non-blocking — the protocol's evidence-based process is the primary quality mechanism.
