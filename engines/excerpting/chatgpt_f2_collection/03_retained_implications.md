# Retained system implications

**Not the owner answer.**  
**Intended for later engineering interpretation.**

## Session entry point
- Rooted tree view, not raw text and not random search.
- Default entry is science → major topic → branch topology.
- The session begins by identifying the exact question before opening excerpts.

## First 5-minute objective
- Build orientation, not consume detail.
- Identify the branch map under the topic.
- Confirm scope, major subdomains, and what belongs elsewhere.
- Detect whether the issue is single-position, multi-position, school-split, proof-rich, or example-rich.

## Preferred navigation order
- Root science
- Major topic
- Sub-branch map
- Exact leaf
- Excerpt-title scan
- Theory excerpts
- Example excerpts
- Proof excerpts
- Return upward to re-anchor

## When I use tree / branch view vs excerpt view
- Tree view first for map, boundaries, relationships, and opinion structure.
- Excerpt view only after the leaf is exact enough.
- Return to tree whenever local detail risks detaching from the whole.

## Theory vs example vs proof usage
- Theory first: establish the exact point, conditions, exceptions, and scope.
- Examples second: turn abstraction into something mentally concrete and test whether understanding is real.
- Proofs third: answer why, compare positions, and prepare for explanation/teaching/defense.
- Proofs are not the entry point; they are opened once the issue is already mapped.

## What must already be pre-solved by the system before study begins
- Topic placement
- Branch relationships
- Scope boundaries
- Cross-science filtering
- School/opinion organization
- Proof grouping
- Example attachment
- Excerpt self-containment
- No manhunt for missing context
- No pollution from neighboring but irrelevant material

## What the library is doing for me during study that traditional study does not
- Gives me the full map before the text
- Solves cross-book gathering in advance
- Solves broad topic-linking in advance
- Makes opinion structure visible before reading
- Makes proof stacks navigable instead of reconstructive
- Lets me descend by exact question rather than by inherited chapter order
- Reduces study from “collect and connect” to “understand and memorize”

## Stopping rules
- I stop descending when the question is exact.
- I stop reading theory when I can state the rule and its limits cleanly.
- I stop examples when they have made the rule concrete.
- I stop proofs when I can explain why the ruling/position stands where it does.
- I stop the subtopic only when the leaf feels structurally stable, not merely familiar.

## Transition rules
- Move down when a branch is too broad.
- Move up when local detail is outrunning the map.
- Move sideways only when a sibling clarifies a boundary.
- Move from theory to examples when the rule is understood verbally but not yet mentally embodied.
- Move from examples to proofs when I now need causation, comparison, or justification.

## Memorization’s role
- Memorization is the remaining human bottleneck after the library has already solved structure and gathering.
- I am not spending my mental energy building the map during study; I am spending it fixing the mapped knowledge into myself.
- Memorization is downstream of clarity, not a substitute for it.

## What counts as a successful 30-minute session
- I know exactly where the studied leaf lives.
- I know what question it answers.
- I can state the ruling or positions.
- I can distinguish it from neighboring branches.
- I have seen enough examples to make it concrete.
- I know where the proofs sit and can open them intentionally.
- I leave with one branch mapped and memorization-ready, not with random scattered reading.
