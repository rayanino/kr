# Title

F-2 — My Study Session

## Immediate reaction

My ideal study session is me locking myself in, opening the library with pen and paper, and letting the library do the structuring, linking, gathering, and filtering before I begin copying one branch of knowledge into my brain. I hate loose knowledge. I want every piece to have a place, then I draw it, repeat it, and recall it.

## Detailed analysis

My ideal study session starts when I go to my room, lock the door, and open the library with pen and paper next to me. I am not opening it just to read. I am opening it to copy one exact part of the science into my head. I start at the tree, not the books. If I am studying prayer, I open الفقه then الصلاة and first skim the scope I want to learn. If the scope for that session contains three branches, I first skim all three. That is my first pass. I want to see where each branch sits, what comes before and after it, how it relates to the rest of the topic, and what the bigger picture is before I go deep into any one part.

As soon as I get one clear understanding, I replicate it on paper. If I understand الفقه → الصلاة, I draw that. If I understand that one branch sits under another, I draw that. I move in very small steps. I do not want knowledge floating in my head without a place. Every piece needs a location: in the tree, on the paper, in the wording, or in some mnemonic or spatial link. So if I need to remember that one leaf comes right after another, I do not want that as loose memory. I try to attach it to something strong in the wording itself or to some other solid mental link, so it sticks and does not disappear.

After the initial skim of the three branches, I go back to the first branch and read it properly until I get a first understanding. Then I do the same for the second and the third. During this phase, I prefer one focused surface at a time. I want to look at one branch, one leaf, one understanding, and master that before moving on. The library should already have solved the heavy work for me before I begin. I should not be worrying whether the excerpting is wrong, whether the taxonomy is wrong, whether the knowledge is corrupted, whether there is enough knowledge, or what science something belongs to. My main job during study should be memorizing and internalizing. Maybe a small part of my effort still goes into making extra connections, but not the main part. The main part should be copying the library into my brain.

Once I cross one relative chunk of related understandings, I stop and summarize from the first point of that chunk until the last. Then I move to the next chunk. At the end of the next chunk, I repeat both: the new one and the previous one. So the session is not linear reading. It is read, understand, draw, connect, summarize, then repeat cumulatively. If I need to refresh something while recalling, then multiple panes help, because at that point I may need to jump between places quickly. But my default learning mode is still focused and one thing at a time.

I do not think of books and audio as separate study worlds. If something belongs to the scope of the topic I am mastering, then it belongs to the session. The medium is secondary. What matters is that the library has already brought everything under the topic into one structured place so I can move through it as one body of knowledge.

After I have covered the understandings I am trying to learn in that pass, I switch over to repetition and challenge. I do not want rereading to be my main method. I want active recall, question and answer, and teaching the point back, even if only to an imaginary student. I want to be challenged on the knowledge. I use rereading more to refresh and check than as the main engine of learning. At the end, I close the library and recall everything by head. That final recall matters because it shows me whether the knowledge has actually transferred or whether it was only recognizable while the screen was open.

So for me, a successful 30-minute session is not that I mastered a whole major branch in half an hour. Full mastery of a large branch can take months, and full scholarly command can take years. A successful 30-minute session is that one real chunk of the tree has moved from screen to paper to memory. I can reproduce it from scratch, place it exactly where it belongs, explain why it is there, and answer basic questions about it without going on a hunt again. The dream is that the library has already solved structure, placement, gathering, and linking, so my study session is mostly the work of turning that order into owned knowledge.

## Confidence

high

## What would change my mind

If in real study I found that this skim → understand → draw → cumulative repetition → recall cycle gave me weaker retention than another pattern, or if I found that I still had to spend a large part of my study time checking whether the library was correct instead of trusting it, I would revise it.

## Principles this reveals

I do not want loose knowledge. I want every piece of knowledge to have a place. The library should already solve structure, gathering, placement, and linkage before I begin, so that my real study session becomes step-by-step transfer from tree to paper to memory through recall.
