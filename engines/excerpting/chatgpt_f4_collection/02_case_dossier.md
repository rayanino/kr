The excerpt as given is:

`واختلف العلماء في حقيقة اليد التي تقطع على أقوال: وأصحها، ما ذهب إليه الجمهور،`

The surrounding context is used only diagnostically:

It must not rescue the owner verdict on the excerpt as given.

# Core assessment thesis

The deepest problem is not mere shortness. The deepest problem is **function fusion plus clipped attribution**.

This short segment contains at least:
1. a disagreement marker
2. a tarjih lead-in

Those are different jobs. The second one is also incomplete in the excerpt as given.

# Why the excerpt as given fails or underperforms

It underperforms for three combined reasons:

1. **It answers two different questions at once**
   - Is there disagreement on this subject?
   - What view does this scholar regard as strongest?

2. **The second function is clipped**
   - `وأصحها، ما ذهب إليه الجمهور،` does not finish the preferred view in the excerpt as given.

3. **The fused packaging hides the difference between an unbiased marker and an attribution-critical judgment**
   - disagreement-marking and tarjih are not interchangeable

# Surface segment vs functional segment

## Surface segment
At surface level, this looks like one short sentence fragment.

## Functional segment
Functionally, it divides into:
- `واختلف العلماء في حقيقة اليد التي تقطع على أقوال:`  
  → mention of disagreement / disagreement marker
- `وأصحها، ما ذهب إليه الجمهور،`  
  → beginning of a scholar’s tarjih
- surrounding continuation: `بل نقل فيه الإجماع، من أنها الكف التي تبتدئ من الكوع،`  
  → completion of the preferred view, available only in the diagnostic context

# Why “mentioning the disagreement” and “tarjih” are not the same job

A disagreement marker answers:
- Is this an issue of khilaf?
- Is this leaf genuinely disputed?

A tarjih answers:
- What view does this scholar prefer?
- Which side is he attributing strength to?

The first is usable as an unbiased signal of division. The second is a scholar-bound judgment.

These should not be flattened into one function merely because they appear adjacent.

# Why the tarjih is attribution-critical

Tarjih is attribution-critical because it is part of what is distinctly attributed to the scholar. Losing or clipping it is not a small formatting issue. It changes what the scholar is being recorded as preferring.

A clipped tarjih is especially dangerous because it gestures toward a judgment without fully stating the judged view.

# Question-cluster analysis

This case is best understood by asking: what question is each segment answering?

## Cluster that should split
- disagreement mention + tarjih

These answer different questions and should not remain fused.

## Cluster that may stay together
- disagreement mention + opinion listing (if listing exists)

These answer the same broader disagreement-structuring question and may be too tightly linked to split.

## Cluster that should stay together
- tarjih marker + explicit completion of the preferred view

The tarjih should not be left as a clipped lead-in.

# Context / `و` analysis

The opening `و` is not, by itself, decisive evidence of an external dependency that must always be repaired in the same way. In this case it may simply continue the broader treatment of the subject.

So the engineering lesson is:
- do not ignore it blindly
- do not overreact to it blindly
- diagnose whether it is an external dependency or a local continuation

# What should split

- disagreement marker from tarjih
- unbiased conflict-marking from attribution-critical preference

# What should remain together

- tarjih lead-in with the explicit statement of the preferred view
- disagreement marker with a full opinion listing, if such a listing genuinely exists in source and remains unbiased

# What surrounding context may be used for

It may be used to:
- confirm that the tarjih is clipped in the excerpt as given
- build a corrected candidate for engineering use
- understand the full function of the scholar’s preference statement

It may not be used to:
- pretend the excerpt as given already works
- upgrade the owner verdict from C to a softer verdict

# What would be reckless to flatten

- treating the whole excerpt as just “an incomplete sentence”
- treating the whole excerpt as only disagreement-marking
- treating the whole excerpt as a full tarjih
- ignoring the attribution sensitivity of the second half
- pretending adjacency means same function

# What would be reckless to automate blindly

- splitting by punctuation alone
- classifying by the first clause only
- treating every opening `و` as the same context type
- letting surrounding context silently repair clipped owner-facing excerpts
- ignoring the question-cluster distinction between disagreement-marking and tarjih

# Additional methodological note

The `excerpt as quote` lens is useful here as a secondary diagnostic. The disagreement marker is quotable on its own without misrepresenting the scholar's act, while the clipped tarjih lead-in is not yet quotable as a completed attributed judgment.
