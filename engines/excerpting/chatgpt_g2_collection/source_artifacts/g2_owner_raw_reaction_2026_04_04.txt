“أنه رَأى عُثْمَانَ دَعَا بِوَضوء” → stop → explain this (what is a ‘وَضوء’ etc)

… → hadith عثمان بن عفان → raw (this is the part where the hadith ready-to-memorize (externally gathered by the library in correct formatting) lives)

But obviously, this introduces more complexity. Rarely do two or three follow the exact same chunking choices when explaining hadiths, let alone tens and hundreds. Also: the longer the hadiths, the more complex this operation becomes.

I just realized: we should define and focus and research the different scenarios of ‘relationships between proofs’. With that I mean: imagine under a specific topic, under its proof leaf there are 3 hadiths present. These hadith have a specific relationship.

For example: they could be ‘supporting each other in order to form the full ruling together’. Such is the case with wudu: it is not 1 hadith that contains the entire ruling, it’s multiple hadiths together that give the full picture of how wudu is to be done.

There are other types of relationships. For example: some hadiths seem contradicting on the first sight, but resolving that contradiction also gives an important part of the ruling, so in some sense they are supporting as well.

In general: proofs being under the same ‘proof-leaf’ should mean that they together support the ruling. It should never happen that two hadiths each supporting a specific opinion are together under the same ‘proof’-leaf; that is not enough granularity.

For example:

“umrah is mandatory” → own branch, with own “proof”-leaf

“umrah is not mandatory” → own branch, with own “proof”-leaf

So we shouldn’t worry about further classification within a proof-leaf regarding “this group of proofs proves opinion1, this group proves opinion2, …” because they are already under a specific opinion-branch. What we should do, however, is clearly indicate the relationship: “proof1 and proof2 both support the opinion that umrah is mandatory, but HOW? Do they each give a piece of the puzzle? Is one a pure saying of the PROPHET, WHILE THE OTHER AN ACTION? ….”

This actually brings me to a broader topic: proofs have much more classification behind them than the library currently accounts for.
