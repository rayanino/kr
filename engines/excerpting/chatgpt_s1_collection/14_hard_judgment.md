# Hard judgment

## Owner-faithful judgment

The strongest owner-faithful ranking is:

1. making the excerpt useful to study from  
2. keeping the exact original text untouched  
3. making the excerpt understandable on its own  
4. making the excerpt the right size  

But that ranking is **not** a clean peer-to-peer ranking.

- **(4)** wins because the system exists to produce teaching units worth studying.
- **(1)** remains near-top and non-negotiable because violating it causes false attribution and corruption.
- **(2)** is the most interpretation-sensitive item: under its ordinary reading it is recoverable context loss; under its harsher reading it becomes deadly.
- **(3)** is not mere brevity; it is granularity fitness and anti-scrambling discipline.

What is solid:
- `4 > 1`

What is less fully settled:
- the exact interpretation and stable ranking behavior of `2` versus `3`

## Broader engineering / protocol judgment

The question itself is structurally asymmetrical. Treating all four items as the same kind of slider would be reckless.

- **(4)** behaves like a top-level objective.
- **(1)** behaves like a hard integrity boundary.
- **(2)** and **(3)** behave like subordinate design pressures.

Reckless flattenings:
- letting usefulness justify mutation
- letting fidelity excuse unusable fragments
- treating “understandable on its own” as a stable single concept
- reducing “right size” to shortness
- pretending the tail of the ranking is as locked as the first two positions

The correct handling is a forced ranking with explicit caveat, not fake neatness.
