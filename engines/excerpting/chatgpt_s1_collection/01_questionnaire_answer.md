**Priority ranking (most to least important):**  
1. making the excerpt useful to study from  
2. keeping the exact original text untouched  
3. making the excerpt understandable on its own  
4. making the excerpt the right size

**Why this order:**  
My strongest answer is that the whole system exists to produce teaching units that are actually worth studying, so making the excerpt useful to study from comes first. But that does **not** mean usefulness is allowed to justify corrupting the source. Keeping the exact original text untouched stays right behind it because silently altering the scholar’s wording, or causing false attribution, is catastrophic. After that comes making the excerpt understandable on its own, but only under the ordinary reading that this means enough context and self-containment that I do not have to hunt manually. That failure is still serious, but less catastrophic than silent corruption because it is not necessarily knowledge-destroying and it is at least in principle recoverable. Last comes making the excerpt the right size, meaning the granularity is fit for study and does not mix topics or scramble the mind. That still matters a lot, but under the current reading it is below the first three.

**Critical caveat / interpretation note:**  
This is a forced ranking over items that are not truly the same kind of thing. “Useful to study from” is more like the top-level purpose. “Keeping the exact original text untouched” is more like a hard integrity boundary. And “understandable on its own” changes its severity depending on what it means: if it only means missing context, then I rank it third; if it means pieces were cut out in a way that damages meaning, then it becomes much more severe and starts collapsing into the same family as corruption. Also, “right size” is not about making excerpts short for their own sake; it is about correct granularity.
