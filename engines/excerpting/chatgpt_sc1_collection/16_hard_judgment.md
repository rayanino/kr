# Owner-faithful judgment

## Final questionnaire choice
**B**

## Why
The strongest owner-faithful answer is not that summary notes are useless in every context. It is that **this summary note is not enough in an excerpt-library setting**.

In normal book reading, this kind of note could work because:
- the earlier discussion is fresh
- the reader can flip back
- the author’s intended flow is still intact

But when the owner opens the unit with zero pre-context, that environment is gone. Then the note becomes insufficient.

## Deepest tension in the raw comments
The central tension is:
- a short reference-back can be sufficient inside a continuously read book
- the same reference-back becomes insufficient after translation into isolated excerpts

That tension must not be flattened into “summary notes are enough” or “summary notes are bad.”

## Strongest owner-faithful interpretation
This specific note is concept-building, not merely orientational. That is why B is stronger than A or C.

## Most important author-flow insight
The author’s intended flow and reader assumptions are part of the meaning environment. Losing them is not a cosmetic loss.

## Most important context-restoration insight
The library must restore the path back itself instead of forcing the owner to search for the earlier passage manually.

## Most important teaching-unit insight
A useful excerpt in this kind of case is no longer just a quote. It becomes a teaching unit — but the source text still remains the center.

## Most important passage-linkage insight
Sometimes the missing support is not another small excerpt but the full original passage or the exact earlier portion being referred to.

## What would be reckless to ignore
- the difference between book-context sufficiency and excerpt-library sufficiency
- the distinction between orientational and concept-building reference-back
- the need for direct passage restoration in cases like this

## What would be reckless to automate blindly
- auto-generating short summary notes and declaring the case solved
- assuming all reference-back notes work the same way
- expanding units without guarding source centrality and discipline

# Broader engineering / protocol judgment

The highest-risk mistake here is to confuse readability with teaching sufficiency.

A summary can read smoothly and still be structurally inadequate if it does not restore the earlier material the author is relying on.

The most important engineering consequence is:
- preserve the source text as the center
- add explicit context restoration around it
- distinguish orientational from concept-building reference-back
- let support escalate from summary note to passage linkage when needed

If this is reduced to a mere note-UX issue, the real structural problem is lost.
