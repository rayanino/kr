# Broader engineering / protocol judgment

## What would be reckless to automate blindly
1. Function classification from chapter-start cues alone.
2. Split decisions from punctuation alone.
3. Title dropping as a universal cleanup step.
4. A pure anti-overlap rule.
5. A pure keep-it-merged rule.

## Highest-risk issue family
**Function misread caused by surface chapter-intro language.**

That is the first failure. Once that happens, everything downstream becomes distorted:
- wrong placement
- wrong split map
- wrong expectations for self-containment
- wrong understanding of what the source is doing

## Why excerpt corruption and misplacement are different here
In this case, the main issue is not that the source text is corrupted. The text itself is present. The danger is a **protocol corruption of function**:
- the wrong excerpt unit
- the wrong home
- the wrong retained context
- the wrong scope interpretation

## Why title retention matters more than it first looks
The title is not just a header. In the intro excerpt it is structurally required because `هذا الباب` depends on it. In the ruling excerpt it becomes contextual evidence about chapter-start placement. Treating title retention as one flat rule would be reckless.

## Why the source-scope issue matters
The first sentence can be mistaken for a full-topic introduction, but it is actually narrowed by the source's chapter agenda. If that distinction is lost, the user can inherit a distorted view of the topic itself.

## Bottom line
The final local verdict is **C**, but the real lesson is not merely “split this excerpt.”  
The real lesson is:

- detect actual function, not surface appearance
- split where functions are genuinely distinct
- keep small linked context where cutting would make the result unsafe
- do not let chapter-specific framing masquerade as full-topic framing
- do not automate any of that by one blunt heuristic
