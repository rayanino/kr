Detailed analysis:
My answer is C, but not because both extremes are equally harmless. The most dangerous failure overall is not taxonomy placement by itself. It is wrong excerpting, knowledge corruption, and especially excerpting-bias. If the system lets the current taxonomy tree influence what gets cut together or split apart, that is far more dangerous than either visible wrong placement or blind messiness. A wrong excerpt is a corruption in knowledge. And to me that is not a small local failure. It puts the entire book that excerpt came from in question, because one wrong excerpt means the structure around it may also be wrong. Then the book cannot be trusted, the excerpting engine cannot be trusted, and my knowledge base becomes unsafe.

A wrong taxonomy leaf is still catastrophic, but it is a different kind of failure. The big difference is that a wrong leaf is much more visible. I can notice that a group of excerpts is sitting in the wrong place, or that a topic was not split deeply enough, or that the structure is awkward. That is severe, but it is easier to spot and easier to reason about. Silent excerpt corruption is much more dangerous because I can study from it for years with confidence and only discover later that the knowledge itself was handled wrongly.

So the real issue for me is where guidance may and may not be used. Excerpting must not be biased by the taxonomy tree. It should be based on the source itself and on what atoms rightfully belong together. If the taxonomy tree is changed—whether more granulated, less granulated, or correctly granulated—the rightful excerpt output should stay exactly the same. If the excerpt output changes merely because the tree changed, that is a dangerous sign that the wrong stage has been contaminated.

Only after excerpting is held correct and independent do I compare the narrower taxonomy failure. Under that narrower comparison, overgranulation worries me more than undergranulation. If the tree is undergranulated, I will usually see that one group of excerpts actually contains two or more real topics, and even though that is disappointing and problematic, it is more detectable and more repairable. But an overgranulated tree is harder to study from because it cuts the science into smaller boxes and forces a harder puzzle: I have to figure out the common denominator of pieces that may have been separated too far. That creates deeper confusion.

So my answer is C. Not because A and B are equal, but because the real danger is guidance in the wrong place. If structure is allowed to bias excerpting, that is more dangerous than the A/B frame itself. Once excerpting is protected, then within the narrower placement comparison, overgranulation feels more dangerous than undergranulation.

What would change my mind:
If it became clear in practice that some prior structure could guide the excerpting stage without ever biasing or changing rightful excerpt outputs, or that blind processing consistently produced worse excerpt integrity than guided processing, I would revisit the balance. But as long as structure can contaminate excerpt truth, I will keep treating that as the real danger.

Principles this reveals:
Excerpt integrity comes before taxonomy comfort. Guidance is not the issue by itself; the issue is where guidance is allowed to influence the pipeline. Visible placement failures and silent excerpt corruption must not be treated as equivalent.
