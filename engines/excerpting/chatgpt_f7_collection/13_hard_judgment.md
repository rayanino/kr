# Hard Judgment

## Owner-faithful judgment

### Failures that would destroy trust immediately
- confirmed corruption in trusted study material
- wrongful excerpting or cutting that changes meaning
- misattribution of knowledge to the wrong scholar or school
- any silent content failure discovered after long trust
- any single discovered error that cannot be cleanly contained and therefore throws all library-derived knowledge into question

### Failures that are survivable but still severe
- loud, bounded defects that are caught before deep study damage
- suspicious mismatches that trigger a full audit
- scale warnings found early enough to redesign before dependence
- recoverable preservation failures that do not destroy the data itself

### Seemingly small failures that are actually unacceptable
- hidden omission
- weak provenance
- one unresolved confidence downgrade presented as normal
- one ranking pattern that repeatedly surfaces the wrong things
- one “small” metadata bug with downstream propagation potential
- one untested backup path

### What would make continued use emotionally or intellectually impossible
- discovering that I studied from the library for years and it silently fed me false knowledge
- discovering that a mistake happened but no one can tell me what else it touched
- discovering that the project was built on loose grounds in a way that permanently capped what my scholarship could have become

## Broader engineering / red-team judgment

### Most dangerous families overall
1. silent corruption and delayed discovery
2. provenance/lineage weakness that prevents containment
3. attribution and quote-layer failure
4. architecture built on loose grounds
5. cost/speed/scale ceilings hidden by successful small runs
6. preservation failure, especially backup theater
7. future dataset contamination for local models
8. prestige illusion created by polished presentation

### Most severe overlooked areas
- the difference between a local defect and a globally trust-poisoning defect
- hidden omission and context loss that still read fluently
- evaluation that looks rigorous but cannot falsify the wrong implementation
- dependency and portability weakness in a lifetime system
- weak storage/lineage choices that only show their damage years later
- any place where the interface can make the owner trust faster than the evidence deserves

### What would be reckless to under-prioritize
- source lineage and versioning
- blast-radius containment
- high-risk excerpt boundary and attribution validation
- corpus-scale economics and latency
- backup restore drills, not just backup existence
- dataset structure for future training
- role/accountability clarity across agents
- research/tooling surveys before locking in foundations

### Bottom line
The library does not fail only when it becomes obviously wrong. It also fails when it becomes subtly unsafe, silently misleading, historically un-auditable, strategically suboptimal, or operationally incapable of reaching the intended corpus and scholarly ceiling.

For this project, “mostly right,” “good enough,” and “we can fix it later” are all dangerous phrases.
