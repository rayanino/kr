The very first thing I notice: the hadith is separated from the sarh. The explained is separated from the explanation. This does not make sense.

I think what the LLM was trying to achieve is: separate the “proof” from the “proving”. And though this makes sense theoretically, it is still problematic in practice, especially in Islamic sciences.

The problem: imagine we have 5 explanation-excerpts, each explaining the hadith of عبد الله ابن عمر. This sounds like a good logical separation of concerns: excerpt the hadith and put it under proofs → hadiths → عبد الله ابن عمر, excerpt the explanations and put them under explanations → hadiths → عبد الله ابن عمر. Something like that.

And I agree that this sounds very good and is exactly the separation I would want. But this is an ideal scenario that will rarely work.
That is because many times, hadith have many versions. A scholar explains one of those versions. Also: hadiths have many different ratings. A scholar explains his hadith keeping that rating in mind. There are just too many factors which make the explanations themselves differ. It’s not like they are explaining only one single source of truth. Though there are enough cases where there is only one version of hadith every scholar explains, there are just too many to take this risk.

Secondly, I don’t want to be hunting: “this explanation from this scholar was based on version x of the hadith”. “this scholar only had access to this wording of the hadith, but I need to look up first which excerpt under proofs → hadiths → عبد الله ابن عمر belongs to him”.

Lastly, it just makes sense in general not to separate an explanation and what it explains. They are just too tightly bonded together.

Keep in mind: this is just what I can think of. I could totally be wrong; there may be a way in which separating explanation and the explained is totally feasible, in which case I would LOVE to have that. I still think the ideal scenario would be to separate the explained from the to-explain … but I’m just afraid of the tradeoffs.

Which is why we have a major to-do waiting: doing research and deep reasoning about if there is a feasibility of putting the main concept the entire library should revolve around (separation of concerns) in practice in cases like explained-explanation, evidence-proof, …

Keep in mind: for the “proofs” part of the library, I would like it not to only have direct one-on-one copies from the books. Books are often loose in the chain, wording, source etc of hadiths. Instead, we should definitely preserve excerpts of proofs as they are found in books, but additionally we need authoritative sources from which hadiths can be fetched to include. This way I can reliably study the structured fetched hadiths, while also having access to how these are mentioned in the books of scholars.

I JUST REALIZED AN IMPORTANT AND POSSIBLY DEADLY THING. It is: a lot of times, “rulings” and “explanations of proofs” are deeply interconnected. Many scholars follow the methodology: topic → proof1 → explanation that at the same time contains the rules → proof2 → more explanations and new rules → …

So … where do we draw the line between “this is an explanation” and this is a “ruling”? Sometimes, scholars bluntly say: ruling (x is haram) → proof1 → explanation that proves the ruling → …

I think I should stop here because I don’t have enough domain knowledge in Islamic sciences to be talking about methodologies of scholars. What I said above could very well be wrong. But I do realize that scholars have many methodologies. WHICH MEANS THE LIBRARY WILL PROBABLY NEVER 100% COVER ALL AND EVERY EDGE CASES, BECAUSE IN THE WORST SCENARIO A NEW SCHOLAR COULD BE BORN, AND I WILL INTAKE A BOOK OF HIS, AND INTRODUCE A NOT BEFORE KNOWN METHODOLOGY!!! WHICH MEANS: FOCUS ON INTELLIGENCE, WISDOM AND REASONING OF THE LLM ITSELF IN EXCERPTING AND TAXONOMY PLACEMENT, ANALYSIS OF THE INDIVIDUAL EXCERPTS INSTEAD OF ONLY RELYING ON THE RULES AND PROTOCOLS WE ARE DEFINING.

So the point is to realize that the more rules and protocols we define, and the more examples, counter examples, edge cases, etc. we define the more accurate the engines get, AND WE SHOULD DO DEEP RESEARCH AND EXTENSIVE DATA ANALYSIS TO RECOGNIZE ALL PATTERNS, but there is still a slim possibility that something new is encountered. Which is why - alongside the many many other security gates the library should have - there should still be security gates related to recognizing cases where the system is not 100% certain of what to do, then making sure to bring that to a good ending.

So a major part of our research and development is being afraid of the unseen: being afraid of the edge cases we do not know exist. AND MORE IMPORTANTLY: NEVER ASSUME EXCERPTING CANNOT GO WRONG. NEVER ASSUME WE HAVE COVERED ALL EDGE CASES. NEVER ASSUME WE SHOULD NOT WORRY ABOUT AN EXCERPT BEING CORRUPTED.
