That is not because the owner thinks the note is a good long-term design. It is because the current pair, as given, does not stand without the note. Without it, the excerpt is an orphaned explanation. So for the forced A/B/C answer, the note is essential and should be shown by default.

The weaker answer would flatten the real issue in two different ways:

- **C** would pretend the excerpt can stand on its own, which is directly against the owner’s stated reaction.

The deepest local failure is not “bad note UX.” It is the earlier structural decision to separate the explanation from what it explains.

The most important explained/explanation insight is this: the owner still likes the *idea* of separating concerns, but in hadith-heavy Islamic sciences the explained material and the explanation are often too tightly linked to separate safely.

The most important note-design insight is this: a generated summary note may be necessary for a broken pair, but that does **not** make it the best remedy.

The most important proof-integrity insight is this: preserving proofs as found in books and fetching authoritative proofs are both necessary; neither layer should erase the other.

The most important methodology-risk insight is this: unseen scholar methodologies and interwoven reasoning patterns make blind rule-only separation unsafe.

What would be reckless to ignore:
- the orphaned-explanation problem

What would be reckless to automate blindly:

# Broader engineering / protocol judgment

The pair is structurally unsound before the note ever enters the picture.

The note solves only the surface symptom: it makes the user less lost. It does not solve the deeper integrity question: whether the explanation is still anchored to the exact source wording, version, route, grading assumptions, and scholar methodology it depends on.

Therefore the engineering judgment is stricter than the questionnaire judgment:

- The current pair earns **A** only as a local forced answer.
- The better design direction is source-preserving context plus explicit proof-layer architecture.
- The most dangerous hidden failure is a readable explanation that is still paired to the wrong or insufficiently specified proof context.
- The most dangerous automation mistake is to take successful note-assisted readability as evidence that the underlying split was valid.

This case should not be treated as “notes are useful.” It should be treated as a warning that the system can create a compensatory note for a structure that may already be wrong.
