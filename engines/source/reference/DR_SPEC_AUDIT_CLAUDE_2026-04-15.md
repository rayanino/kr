# Islamic text patterns for Arabic library pipeline design

**Arabic Islamic texts exhibit at least seven structural patterns that break standard bibliographic assumptions**, and no existing digital library — Shamela, OpenITI, KITAB, or Western catalogs — handles all of them adequately. The core challenge is that Islamic scholarly tradition developed its own complex system of derivative relationships (mukhtasar → sharh → hashiyah chains up to five levels deep), composite works (majmu' collections bundling dozens of independent risalahs), multi-layered documents (tafsir with 10+ interwoven content types), and contributor roles (إعداد, تحقيق, جمع وترتيب) that map poorly onto MARC, FRBR, or Dublin Core. A source engine spec must model these patterns natively or risk collapsing critical distinctions that Islamic scholars and readers depend on.

## Majmu' collections demand whole-part decomposition

The pattern of bundling independent risalahs into a single "book" is pervasive in Islamic digital libraries. **مجموع فتاوى ابن تيمية** (Majmu' Fatawa Ibn Taymiyyah) — the paradigmatic example — contains hundreds of independent works: complete books, treatises, and individual fatwas authored by Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 728 AH), compiled posthumously by ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Muḥammad ibn Qāsim into **37 volumes plus indices**. The compilation is thematically organized (Volume 1 covers Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah, Volumes 5–6 cover al-Asma' wa al-Sifat, Volume 9 covers Logic), and the official index is titled "Index of the books, treatises, and questions deposited in Majmu' Fatawa." Each constituent piece — such as al-ʿAqīdah al-Wāsiṭiyyah — circulates both independently and within the Majmu'.

**مجموع رسائل ابن رجب** follows the same structure: 4 volumes containing **30+ independent risalahs** spanning tawhid, fiqh, tafsir, hadith, and zuhd, compiled by Abū Muṣʿab al-Ḥulwānī, each risalah collated against original manuscripts (some against seven witnesses). Similar collections exist for Ibn ʿUthaymin (مجموع فتاوى ورسائل العثيمين, 26–35 volumes) and al-Albani.

Both Shamela and OpenITI treat the entire majmu' as a single entry. Shamela records Majmu' al-Fatawa as Book #7289 with continuous page numbering. OpenITI assigns a single URI (`0728IbnTaymiyya.MajmuFatawa.Shamela0007289`) and stores it as one text file. Individual risalahs exist only as internal table-of-contents entries, not as separate work records. OpenITI's YML metadata files can note parts within manuscripts but lack systematic sub-work decomposition. The practical recommendation for a pipeline spec is to create a **work record for the majmu'** (with compiler as contributor) plus **separate work records for each identifiable risalah**, linked via `hasPart`/`isPartOf` relationships with volume and page coordinates.

## The إعداد role is neither author nor editor

Six distinct contributor roles appear on Arabic Islamic title pages, and conflating them corrupts metadata. Based on the IslamWeb fatwa (#259582) distinguishing these terms and real examples from Shamela and the IDEO catalog:

**تأليف (ta'lif)** designates original intellectual authorship — composing new content. **تصنيف (tasnif)** is classification-based composition within a single discipline. **تحقيق (tahqiq)** is critical editing: establishing correct text from manuscripts, adding apparatus. **جمع وترتيب (jam' wa tartib)** is collecting and arranging materials from multiple sources. **إعداد (i'dad)** literally means "preparation/readying" — the person credited did not create original content or establish text from manuscripts, but prepared, organized, and arranged existing material for publication. **اعتناء (i'tina')** indicates light editorial oversight.

Real title-page examples reveal how these combine: the edition of *Sharh al-Tibi ʿala Mishkat al-Masabih* credits "إعداد مركز الدراسات والبحوث بمكتبة نزار الباز" (preparation by a research center) alongside "تحقيق ودراسة عبد الحميد هنداوي" (critical editing by a named scholar). The Arabic edition of Gacek's *The Arabic Manuscript Tradition* separates "تأليف آدم جاسك" (authoring), "إعداد محمود محمد زكي" (preparation), and "مراجعة فيصل الحفيان" (review). Institutional إعداد credits are common for compilations like Kuwait's *مدونة أحكام الوقف الفقهية*.

For the pipeline, the person credited with إعداد should be classified as **"preparer" (معد)** — a distinct role, not author, not editor, not compiler. Shamela's metadata uses specific labels (المؤلف for author, المحقق for critical editor) and records إعداد when present. A metadata schema should support at minimum these role types: `mu'allif` (author), `muhaqqiq` (critical editor), `mu'idd` (preparer), `jami'` (collector), `mushrif` (supervisor), `mu'tani` (caretaker), and `mukharrij` (hadith verifier).

## Mixed-format PDFs follow a predictable three-zone pattern

PDFs of Islamic texts routinely mix typeset and scanned content, following a structure so consistent it can be detected programmatically. The standard tahqiq edition produces three visual zones:

**Zone 1 — Typed muqaddima** (20–100+ pages): The editor's introduction, author biography, manuscript witness descriptions, and methodology. Entirely computer-typeset, OCR-friendly. **Zone 2 — Typed critical text** with apparatus: The established text with footnotes containing variant readings, manuscript sigla (أ، ب، ج), and cross-references. Smaller typeface in footnotes, specialized symbols, mixed Arabic/Latin characters. **Zone 3 — Scanned manuscript facsimiles** (ملحق صور المخطوط): Photographic reproductions of key manuscript pages — title page, opening, colophon, and problematic passages. Requires HTR (handwritten text recognition), not OCR.

University thesis editions (رسائل جامعية) — the most common mixed-format source — add a fourth zone: the academic study section (القسم الدراسي, 50–200 pages) preceding the critical text. These theses universally require manuscript appendices for authentication, and when digitized as single PDFs, the visual transition between zones occurs without structural markers.

OpenITI addresses this through its mARkdown scheme: the `### |EDITOR|` tag explicitly marks title pages, front matter, introductions, and indices, separating editorial content from the original text. The OpenITI Arabic-script OCR pipeline (AOCP) uses the Kraken engine with eScriptorium, training models by aligning existing OpenITI transcriptions with scanned pages via the *passim* text-reuse detection tool. For manuscript pages specifically, OpenITI's MSS repository tracks folio numbering (`FolioV01P001A` for recto, `FolioV01P001B` for verso) and marginal texts with URI infixes. A pipeline should implement **page-level image classification** to detect these zones automatically — resolution and noise characteristics differ sharply between typeset and manuscript pages.

## Nazm-with-sharh editions use four distinct embedding formats

Islamic didactic versification (نظم) reached its golden age during the Mamluk period and produced the canonical curriculum texts memorized across the Muslim world. The major examples span every Islamic science:

- **ألفية ابن مالك** (Alfiyyat Ibn Malik, d. 672 AH): ~1,000 verses covering Arabic grammar, with **43+ known commentaries** — the most commented didactic poem in Arabic
- **نونية ابن القيم** (Nuniyyat Ibn al-Qayyim, d. 751 AH): ~5,800 verses on creed
- **المنظومة البيقونية** (al-Bayquniyyah): 34 verses on hadith terminology — an ultra-concise beginner text
- **الجزرية** (al-Jazariyyah, d. 833 AH): 107 verses on tajweed rules
- **مراقي السعود** (Maraqi al-Su'ud): ~1,000 verses on usul al-fiqh, standard in the Mauritanian/Maliki tradition

When printed with commentary, four layout formats appear. **Format A (interlinear/integrated, الشرح الممزوج)** is dominant: the sharh quotes one or a few verses, then provides explanation. The matn is distinguished typographically via bold, color, brackets, or font size — for example, Sharh Ibn ʿAqil on the Alfiyyah prints each verse in bold followed by regular-weight commentary. **Format B (separated)** prints the entire nazm first, then commentary in a separate section. **Format C (marginal/hashiyah)** places the main text centrally with commentary in the margins, common in lithograph editions and creating severe OCR challenges from multi-directional text flow. **Format D (mazj/absorption)** weaves the matn words grammatically into the sharh prose, marking them typographically but integrating them syntactically.

A metadata system needs two orthogonal classification axes: **literary form** (`verse` / `prose` / `mixed`) and **intellectual role** (`matn` / `sharh` / `hashiyah` / `taliqa`). Additionally, when a sharh on a nazm is the primary work, the metadata must record the `matn_embedding_style` (`interlinear` | `separated` | `marginal` | `mazj`) and the `typographic_distinction` method. Shamela can link sharh books to their matn counterparts but does **not** provide fine-grained markup distinguishing quoted matn verses from sharh prose within an integrated commentary — this gap must be addressed by automated detection of typographic patterns.

## Anonymous and pseudepigraphic works require confidence-level attribution

Genuinely anonymous works and false attributions are more common in the Islamic corpus than most metadata systems acknowledge. Key examples of true anonymity include **كتاب المباني لنظم المعاني** (a Quranic sciences work long classified as مجهول المؤلف until a 1426 AH study attempted identification), **أخبار سلاجقة الروم** (Shamela records the author as "مجهول - من أهل القرن السابع الهجري"), and the **Arabic recension of Kalila wa-Dimna** (no extant manuscript predates the 7th/13th century, with multiple anonymous redactions — the focus of OpenITI's AnonymClassic project).

Pseudepigraphy is rampant. Of **several hundred works attributed to al-Ghazali**, only about five dozen are plausibly identifiable; disputed titles include *al-Radd al-Jamil*, *Minhaj al-ʿAbidin*, *al-Madnun*, and *al-Risala al-Laduniyya*. Of **850 works attributed to Ibn al-ʿArabi**, only ~700 are considered authentic and ~400 are extant; falsely attributed texts include *al-Shajara al-Nuʿmaniyya*, an eschatological work prophesying Ottoman sultans. The Library of Congress catalog contains numerous "Anonymous commentary on..." entries for Islamic legal texts.

Shamela handles anonymous works through a dedicated author entry: **"مجهولون"** (Unknowns) at `shamela.ws/author/2610`, aggregating all anonymous works. Individual entries record "المؤلف: مجهول" with optional temporal qualifiers ("من أهل القرن السابع الهجري"). OpenITI's URI system requires a death date and author name (`0728IbnTaymiyya`), creating a structural problem for anonymous texts — no documented convention exists for truly unknown authors, though disputed works use the conventionally attributed author with metadata notes. The pipeline should support **attribution confidence levels**: confirmed, disputed (منسوب إلى), anonymous (مجهول المؤلف), pseudepigraphic, and collective — never binary author/no-author.

## Mukhtasar chains create derivative networks up to five levels deep

Islamic bibliography's richest structural feature is the derivative work chain. The Maliki fiqh tradition illustrates the full depth: the **Mudawwana** (Sahnun from Imam Malik) generated Ibn al-Hajib's Mukhtasar (d. 646 AH), which Khalil first commented on in *al-Tawdih*, then abridged into **Mukhtasar Khalil** (d. 776 AH, 348 known manuscripts), which spawned at least six major commentaries (al-Mawwaq, al-Hattab, al-Dardiri, al-Zarqani, ʿUlaysh, and others), each of which generated supercommentaries — al-Dardiri's Sharh al-Kabir produced **Hashiyat al-Dusuqi**, al-Zarqani's Sharh produced **Hashiyat al-Bannani**. Al-Dardiri also further abridged Mukhtasar Khalil into *Aqrab al-Masalik*, then wrote a commentary on his own abridgement (*al-Sharh al-Saghir*), which generated Hashiyat al-Sawi. That is five levels: original → mukhtasar → sharh → mukhtasar-of-mukhtasar → sharh-on-mukhtasar → hashiyah.

The Shafi'i chain is equally deep: al-Rafi'i's *al-Muharrar* → al-Nawawi's *Minhaj al-Talibin* → Ibn Hajar al-Haytami's *Tuhfat al-Muhtaj* → Hashiyat al-Sharwani.

FRBR maps these relationships: mukhtasar = **derivative** (abridgement creates a new Work), sharh = **descriptive** (commentary creates a new Work with a descriptive relationship), hashiyah = another descriptive link. But Shamela does **not** formally link derivative works — each book is an independent entry with no clickable connections to its source or derivative texts. WorldCat treats mukhtasars as separate creative works with both the abridger and original author recorded, but does not model chain relationships. Western catalogs using MARC/RDA are "largely flat" and cannot represent multi-level derivative chains. The pipeline needs typed work-to-work relationships: `isAbridgementOf`, `isCommentaryOn`, `isSupercommentaryOn`, `isVersificationOf`, with chain traversal capability.

## Tafsir works contain at least ten identifiable structural layers

Beyond the basic Quranic text + commentary structure, classical tafsirs embed numerous distinct content types that a metadata system should recognize:

**Layer 1 — Quranic text (آيات):** Verse segments presented as the object of interpretation, indexed by surah:ayah. **Layer 2 — Commentary proper (تفسير):** The mufassir's exegetical analysis. **Layer 3 — Hadith isnads (أسانيد):** Chains of narration; the KITAB project extracted **32,115 instances** of the ḥaddathanī/ḥaddathanā formula across al-Tabari's works alone. **Layer 4 — Qira'at (قراءات):** Variant Quran readings; OpenITI's corpus contains 29 qira'at texts totaling 2.4 million words. **Layer 5 — I'rab (إعراب):** Grammatical parsing, dominant in al-Zamakhshari's *al-Kashshaf*. **Layer 6 — Asbab al-nuzul (أسباب النزول):** Occasions of revelation. **Layer 7 — Nasikh wa mansukh (ناسخ ومنسوخ):** Abrogation status. **Layer 8 — Fiqhi rulings (أحكام):** Legal deductions, dominant in al-Qurtubi's *al-Jami' li-Ahkam al-Quran*. **Layer 9 — Poetry citations (شواهد شعرية):** Pre-Islamic and early Islamic poetry as linguistic evidence; a "notable feature of Tabari's own commentary." **Layer 10 — Israeliyyat (إسرائيليات):** Narratives from Jewish/Christian tradition.

Different tafsirs weight these layers dramatically differently. Al-Tabari (d. 310 AH) is isnad-heavy, presenting multiple chains for each interpretive report, then offering tarjih (preference). Al-Qurtubi (d. 671 AH) organizes by legal masā'il derived from each verse, presenting cross-school opinions. Al-Zamakhshari (d. 538 AH) foregrounds grammatical and rhetorical analysis with minimal isnads — "the first to place rhetorical analysis at the heart of interpreting the Quranic text." Al-Jalalayn is ultra-concise, essentially providing verse-by-verse glossing with minimal sub-layers. Ibn Ashur's modern *al-Tahrir wa al-Tanwir* adds literary analysis and maqasid al-shari'a reasoning.

No existing digital system fully tags these layers. Shamela stores tafsir as **flat, unstructured text** in Microsoft Access `.mdb` files (renamed to `.bok`), with basic page numbering aligned to print editions. The "Golden Edition" links Quranic verses to 112 classical tafasir but only at verse level, not at sub-layer level. Quran.com's API stores tafsir text as **flat HTML blobs** per verse — no structural distinction between isnads, fiqh rulings, and poetry within a commentary entry. OpenITI comes closest with its riwāyāt tagging: `# $RWY$` marks each hadith/riwaya, `@MATN@` separates isnad from matn content, and `@HUKM@` marks evaluation statements. However, this tagging is not systematically applied to all **207 tafsir texts** (84 million words) in the OpenITI corpus. KITAB's computational approach — using the passim algorithm to detect text reuse and isnad formulas via regex (`\bو?(عن|حدث(نا|ني|ت)|[اأ]نبأ(نا|ني|ت)|...)`) — demonstrates that semi-automatic layer identification is feasible and should inform pipeline design.

## Conclusion

These seven patterns converge on a single architectural insight: **Islamic texts are fundamentally relational and multi-layered**, and treating them as flat, atomic "books" (as Shamela and most systems do) loses essential structure. The pipeline spec should implement five capabilities that no existing system fully provides. First, **composite work decomposition** with `hasPart`/`isPartOf` for majmu' → risalah relationships. Second, **typed derivative chains** supporting at least five levels of mukhtasar → sharh → hashiyah linkage. Third, **role-granular contributor metadata** distinguishing تأليف, تحقيق, إعداد, جمع وترتيب, and اعتناء as first-class role types. Fourth, **multi-layer content annotation** for tafsir and similar genres, marking isnads, qira'at, i'rab, and fiqh rulings as distinct semantic layers within the commentary stream. Fifth, **confidence-graded attribution** supporting confirmed, disputed, anonymous, and pseudepigraphic states. OpenITI's mARkdown scheme and KITAB's text-reuse detection provide the strongest existing foundations to build on, while Shamela's massive corpus (45% of OpenITI's texts) remains the essential source — but its flat `.bok` format is the primary structure that the pipeline must transcend.
